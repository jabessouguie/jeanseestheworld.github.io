Eating in Naples: The Guide Without the Romanticism

Pizza, sfogliatella, caffè con cremina — what to order, where, and what it actually costs

Naples has a food culture that is genuinely distinct from everywhere else in Italy — not in the way travel writing says so about every city, but in actual observable ways. The pizza dough is fermented for 24–48 hours and baked at higher temperature than anywhere up north, which produces a centre so wet it sits like a soft puddle. Coffee is cheaper here than in Milan by 30–40 cents and the caffè con cremina doesn't exist outside the city in quite the same form. The sfogliatella — either the crispy layered shell or the short-pastry version — is sold warm from the oven and costs €2.50. I spent two full days eating my way through the centro storico and the Rione Sanità, and this is what I found worth your time and money, and what I'd skip on the next trip.

Pizza: what makes the Neapolitan version different

The dough is long-fermented — anywhere from 24 to 72 hours depending on the maker. That fermentation changes the texture: it's lighter, more extensible, slightly tangy. The centre of a proper Neapolitan pizza is intentionally soft and wet — what locals call "alveolato" (with air pockets) at the rim and fluid in the middle. If you're expecting a sturdy Roman-style base, this will surprise you.

Portafoglio — folding the slice in four like a wallet — is how Neapolitans eat pizza on the go. It's not a tourist affectation. Many street spots sell pizza by the slice specifically for walking, and folding is the only practical option given the wet centre.

The tourist trap

The pizzerias around Piazza del Plebiscito with laminated menus, photos of every pizza, and a host waving you in from the street are worth skipping. They're trading on location rent, not dough quality. Walk ten minutes north into the centro storico and you pay the same price for a substantially better pizza.

Pizza fritta: the other pizza

Pizza fritta is a fried calzone — dough folded around a filling of ricotta, provola (smoked mozzarella) and sometimes salami or ciccioli, then dropped in hot oil. The result is a crispy, golden casing with a molten filling. It costs around €5 and is substantial enough to count as a lunch on its own.

The most useful address in the centro storico: Sorbillo Antica Pizza Fritta da Zia Esterina, Via dei Tribunali 35. It's the fried pizza counter attached to the Gino Sorbillo pizzeria — a separate entrance, shorter queue, and the quality is consistent. You order standing, pay at the register, eat on the street.

Tip

Pizza fritta is best eaten immediately. The casing softens quickly once it cools. Order and eat on the spot rather than wrapping it to take away.

Sfogliatella and the morning pastry ritual

Sfogliatella comes in two forms and both are worth trying:

  • Riccia: thin, crispy, layered pastry shell — like rough filo wrapped into a cone shape. Flaky and slightly chewy. This is the version most people mean when they say "sfogliatella".
  • Frolla: smooth, shortcrust pastry. Softer, more biscuit-like, less dramatic to look at. Equally good in a different way.

Both are filled with ricotta, semolina, candied citrus peel and cinnamon. Both cost around €2.50. Any decent bar or pasticceria in the centro storico will have them — ask for "una riccia e una frolla" and you'll be eating breakfast for €5.

Caffè con cremina

This is a short espresso served with a thick mousse on top, made by whipping a small amount of cooled espresso with sugar until it becomes a dense cream. The result is intensely sweet and slightly bitter — it's not a latte, it's more like a coffee meringue on top of your espresso. Order by saying "un caffè con cremina" at any bar. It costs €1.20–1.50 and is a Naples-specific thing — I've never found quite the same version in Rome or Florence.

Fiocco di neve at Poppella

In the Rione Sanità neighbourhood: Poppella, Via Arena della Sanità 24. Their signature is the fiocco di neve — a round brioche bun filled with ricotta cream and dusted with powdered sugar. €2. The brioche itself is pillowy and slightly sweet. This is a short walk from the Catacombe di San Gennaro if you're doing that neighbourhood in the afternoon.

Tandem Ragù: the one-dish lunch

Tandem Ragù at Via Giovanni Paladino 51 serves one thing: ragù Napoletano — a slow-cooked meat sauce that simmers for hours until the tomato caramelises and the meat falls apart. You choose your pasta shape. The room is small. The price for a full plate with a glass of wine is around €15–18.

They take reservations via Instagram DMs — search for @tandem_ragù. Outside summer, 2–3 days ahead is sufficient. In July and August, book a week in advance. Lunch slots (12:30–14:30) are slightly easier to get than dinner.

Practical note

Tandem is not a restaurant in the tourist-zone sense — no English menu, no photos on the walls. The staff speak some English. Point at what the next table is having if in doubt. It's the same dish for everyone anyway.

For context: a mid-range dinner at a sit-down restaurant (antipasto, primo, glass of wine) in the centro storico runs €25–35 per person. Naples is one of the cheaper cities in Italy for food — you eat better here for less money than in Milan or Florence.

Frequently asked questions

What is portafoglio pizza and is it acceptable to eat pizza that way in Naples?

Portafoglio means folding the pizza slice in four like a wallet so you can eat it while walking. It's a completely standard way to eat pizza in Naples — street vendors and many pizzerias sell it that way by default. The centre of a proper Neapolitan pizza is intentionally wet and floppy, so folding it is practical, not disrespectful.

What is the difference between sfogliatella riccia and sfogliatella frolla?

Sfogliatella riccia has a shell of thin, crispy, layered pastry — flaky and slightly chewy. Sfogliatella frolla has a smooth shortcrust pastry shell — softer and more biscuit-like. Both are filled with ricotta and semolina. Try both: they are different enough to justify it. Price is the same, around €2.50 each.

What is caffè con cremina and how do I order it?

Caffè con cremina is a Naples speciality: a short espresso topped with a thick, sweet mousse made from whipping sugar and a small amount of espresso together. To order, say 'un caffè con cremina' at any bar in Naples. It costs €1.20–1.50. This is not found in the same form outside Naples.

How do I book Tandem Ragù in Naples?

Tandem Ragù (Via Giovanni Paladino 51) takes reservations via their Instagram DMs. They serve only one dish — a slow-cooked ragù Napoletano — in a small room with a handful of tables. Booking 2–3 days ahead is enough outside summer; in July and August aim for a week in advance. Lunch is the safer slot to get a table.

What is a realistic food budget for a day in Naples?

A realistic daily food budget in Naples: espresso €1.20, sfogliatella €2.50, pizza fritta €5, a sit-down lunch with wine €15–20. Total for a full eating day with those four items: around €24–29. You can eat very well in Naples for €30 a day if you eat standing at the bar for breakfast and buy street food for snacks.
