Island-Hopping from Split: Blue Lagoon, Vis & Hvar

The beaches inside Split are fine. But the real reason to base yourself there is everything reachable by boat in a single day.

Croatia — Dalmatian Coast Full day (10–11 h) €100–130 Best: June–Sept

Split has a beach. Several, actually. Bačvice is the most famous — a shallow sandy bay 10 minutes' walk from the old town, where Croatians play a bat-and-ball game called picigin in knee-deep water. I spent about 45 minutes there, decided it was pleasant but nothing I needed to repeat, and went to book a boat tour instead. The following day I was in the Blue Lagoon at 09:30, snorkelling in water clear enough to see the bottom at 12 metres, and by 14:00 I was eating lunch in Komiža on Vis, watching the fishing boats. That day cost me €115. It was the best money I spent in Croatia.

How to choose between the tours

When you walk along Split's Riva promenade, you'll be approached by at least five people selling boat tours. They all sound similar. They're not.

The stops in detail

Blue Cave (Biševo) — 08:30–10:00

The boat leaves Split at around 07:00 and heads directly to Biševo, the furthest island (about 2 hours sailing). The Blue Cave entry is by small rowboat, 5–6 people at a time, and takes about 5 minutes inside. The bioluminescent blue light is real — it comes from sunlight filtering through an underwater opening — but it only works when the sun is at the right angle, between roughly 10:00 and 12:00. Boats time their arrival accordingly. If the sea is rough, this stop gets skipped and the cost is partially refunded.

Blue Lagoon — 10:00–11:30

A sheltered bay between the small islands of Drvenik Veli and Budikovac. The water is very shallow (1–3 metres at the edges) and very clear. This is where the snorkelling gear comes out. Temperature in July–August is around 24–26°C. The boat anchors for 60–90 minutes. Bring your own waterproof phone case — yours will be the only one not dropping to the bottom.

Vis (Komiža) — 12:00–13:30

Vis has been inhabited continuously since ancient Greece. Komiža, on the western side, is the fishing town — old stone buildings, no cruise ships, a ruined castle above the harbour. The contrast with the rest of the day is sharp: quiet backstreets, a good bakery (Pekara Komiža, ask locals for the current best one), and the feeling that not everyone here is a tourist. Lunch here is €15–20 for a proper meal. I'd prioritise it over Hvar for eating.

Hvar Town — 14:00–16:00

Hvar is the opposite of Vis. Beautiful old town, Venetian architecture, excellent fortress — and also €5 bottles of water on the Riva, €30 lunch plates, and enough tourists to fill every terrace. That doesn't make it not worth seeing; the Španjola fortress is 20 minutes of uphill walking from the main square and gives you a view over the islands that's hard to get elsewhere. Do that instead of sitting at a bar on the waterfront.

Hvar tip

The fortress (Tvrđava Španjola) entry is around €10. Walk up in the morning when it's cooler — though on a boat tour you'll typically arrive in the afternoon. Bring your own water from the boat rather than buying on the promenade.

Skip Split's beaches — put the budget in the boat

I want to be direct about this: if you have one day to spend in Split beyond arriving, walking Diocletian's Palace, and exploring the old town, spend it on a boat tour. Not on the beaches.

Bačvice is worth 45 minutes. Kasjuni beach, a 30-minute bus ride south, is nicer. Neither competes with swimming in the Blue Lagoon or the clear water around Vis. The ferry to the islands from Split costs €15–20 return per island independently. A full tour at €115 covers 5 islands including entry fees and transport. The maths isn't close.

Frequently asked questions

Which boat tour from Split is the best?

The "Blue Cave + 5 Islands" tour is the most complete option: it visits the Blue Cave on Biševo, the Blue Lagoon near Budikovac, Vis (Komiža), Hvar, and Stari Grad. It costs €100–130 and typically includes snorkelling gear. Avoid the cheaper 3-island tours — they rush the stops and often skip Vis entirely.

What is the difference between Vis and Hvar?

Vis (specifically Komiža) is quieter, more lived-in, and genuinely local. Fishing boats, older stone buildings, no club music during the day. Hvar town is the opposite: bars, yachts, tourists everywhere, and prices to match. A bottle of water in Hvar costs €5 on the waterfront. Both are worth seeing on the same tour to feel the contrast.

Is snorkelling included in island-hopping tours?

Most "Blue Cave + 5 Islands" tours include basic snorkelling gear (mask and fins). The Blue Lagoon stop is where people actually use it. Water visibility is excellent at 10–15 metres. Bring a waterproof bag for your phone.

Can I visit Hvar from Split without a boat tour?

Yes. Jadrolinija ferries run Split–Hvar (Stari Grad) year-round in about 2 hours, and there's a fast catamaran to Hvar town in roughly 1 hour. But combining it with the other stops on a day tour gives much better value unless you're planning a multi-day stay on Hvar.

Is the Blue Cave on Biševo worth the extra cost?

Yes, with caveats. The blue glow effect is real and impressive, but it only works between about 10:00 and 12:00 when sunlight enters through an underwater opening. The cave can be closed in rough weather. If the sea is choppy, boats skip Biševo and refund the cave portion.
