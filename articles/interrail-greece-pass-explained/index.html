The Interrail Pass in Greece: Is It Worth It?

Crete has no trains, ferries are not covered, but Athens–Meteora is a solid use — here is the actual maths

Greece Interrail Year-round

Greece comes up frequently when people ask about Interrail itineraries, and the question is almost always the same: does the pass actually make financial sense here? The short answer is: it depends entirely on what you are combining Greece with. The Greek rail network is limited — useful for a handful of routes, invisible for others — and the most expensive leg of most Greek itineraries (the ferry to Crete) is not covered at all. That does not make the pass useless in Greece. The Athens–Meteora train alone costs €28–35 without a pass and is covered with a pass travel day. But if you are only doing Greece, the maths often does not work. Here is the full breakdown.

The TrainOSE network: what exists and what does not

Greek railways are operated by TrainOSE. The network is genuinely limited compared to France, Germany, or Italy — it covers a few main corridors and nothing more.

Main line: Athens–Thessaloniki–Larissa

The backbone of the Greek rail network. Athens (Larissa Station) to Thessaloniki takes 4–5 hours on the fastest intercity services. This route is well-used, reliable, and fully covered by Interrail. Reservation fees apply (€5–10 per journey depending on service class).

Branch to Kalambaka (Meteora)

The Larissa–Kalambaka branch gives you direct train access to the Meteora monasteries. The Athens–Kalambaka direct train takes 4h30 and costs €28–35 without a pass. With a pass it costs only the reservation fee (~€5). This is the single best Interrail value in Greece for most travellers.

What does not exist

  • Crete: no railway network at all. Getting to the island requires a ferry or plane.
  • Mount Olympus: the nearest station is Larissa (approx. 80km away). From Larissa, you need a bus or taxi to Litochoro (the base village for Olympus hikes). The train covers the Larissa leg; the last mile does not.
  • Most Greek islands: none are reachable by train.
  • Peloponnese: the old narrow-gauge network was partially dismantled. Limited TrainOSE services exist but schedules are infrequent.
Network reality check

If your Greek itinerary involves Athens, Thessaloniki, and Meteora, the pass has clear uses. If it involves Crete, the islands, or the Peloponnese, the pass contributes very little to your Greek travel costs.

Ferries: what Interrail covers (and does not)

Athens (Piraeus) to Crete: NOT covered

The overnight ferry from Piraeus to Heraklion or Chania is operated by ANEK Lines and Minoan Lines. Neither participates in the Interrail ferry discount scheme. A cabin on the Athens–Crete ferry costs €35–50 each way. This is a fixed cost regardless of your pass. Budget it separately.

Attica Group (Superfast/Blue Star): partial discount

The Attica Group, which operates Superfast Ferries and Blue Star Ferries, offers Interrail holders a 30% discount on some routes. These routes include Athens–Ancona (Italy) and some Aegean island connections. The discount is not guaranteed on all sailings and must be booked through specific channels — check the current Interrail partner page before assuming coverage.

Practical implication

Do not factor ferry coverage into your pass value calculation for a typical Greece itinerary. Assume all ferry costs are additional. The discount, if you get it, is a bonus.

The maths: when does the pass pay off?

Pass cost: Global Flexi 4 days

A Global Flexi pass with 4 travel days within a 1-month window costs approximately €212 for an adult (26+). Youth (under 28) passes are cheaper — approximately €159. Prices vary and should be verified at interrail.eu.

What 4 travel days in Greece cover

Journey Without pass With pass (+ reservation) Saving
Athens → Kalambaka €28–35 €5 (reservation) €23–30
Kalambaka → Thessaloniki €20–28 €5 (reservation) €15–23
Athens → Thessaloniki €35–50 €5–10 (reservation) €25–45
Athens → Larissa (Olympus) €20–28 €5 (reservation) €15–23
Greece-only total savings (4 journeys) €103–136 €20–25 €78–111

With 4 Greek train journeys, the Greece-only savings are €78–111. The pass costs €212. The pass does not pay for itself on a Greece-only trip.

When it does pay off: combining with other countries

A Global Flexi pass covers 33 countries. If you are travelling Greece as part of a broader European trip — say, arriving via Italy and continuing to the Balkans — the accumulated savings across multiple countries change the calculation entirely. Athens–Rome by Superfast Ferry + Italian trains, or a Balkans pass combined with Greece, can push the savings past the pass cost.

Honest verdict

If Greece is your only Interrail destination: buy point-to-point tickets. The maths does not work. If Greece is one stop on a multi-country trip: the pass adds real value on the Athens–Meteora leg and reduces ferry costs on Italy connections. Buy the pass for the trip, not just for Greece.

Practical tips for using the pass in Greece

Reservations are mandatory on intercity trains

TrainOSE intercity services require a seat reservation even for pass holders. The reservation fee is €5–10. Book through the TrainOSE website or at Athens Larissa Station. Do not book on the day for the Athens–Kalambaka train in high season — it sells out.

Use travel days strategically

With a Flexi pass you choose which days to activate a travel day. A travel day covers midnight to midnight. If your Athens–Kalambaka train departs at 08:00 and your Kalambaka–Thessaloniki train departs the following day, those are two separate travel days.

The Rail Planner app is your route tool

Download it before you go. It works offline. Search TrainOSE routes and check which services require reservation. Not all regional trains in Greece require one (commuter trains around Athens do not), but intercity trains always do.

Ferry booking for Crete

Book the Athens–Crete ferry separately, directly on the ANEK or Minoan Lines websites. Book at least 2 weeks ahead in July–August for cabins, which sell out. The open deck (airline-style seat or deck seat) always has space but is 5–9 hours overnight — not comfortable.

Frequently asked questions

Does the Interrail pass cover trains in Greece?

Yes, but the Greek rail network (TrainOSE) is limited. The main useful line for travellers is Athens–Thessaloniki–Larissa, with a branch to Kalambaka (Meteora). The pass covers all TrainOSE trains but reservation fees (€5–10 per journey) are required on intercity services.

Does the Interrail pass cover ferries in Greece?

The Athens–Crete ferry (ANEK/Minoan Lines) is NOT covered by Interrail. The Attica Group (Superfast Ferries, Blue Star Ferries) offers a 30% discount on some routes to Interrail holders, but not free travel. Always check the current discount status at interrail.eu before assuming coverage.

Is there a train to Crete?

No. Crete has no railway network. Getting to the island requires a ferry (5–9 hours overnight from Piraeus) or a short flight from Athens (45 minutes). Neither is covered by Interrail — ferries are not covered and flights are never covered.

Can I get to Meteora by train with an Interrail pass?

Yes. The Athens–Kalambaka direct train takes 4h30 and is fully covered by the Interrail pass (plus a reservation fee of around €5). The same ticket without a pass costs €28–35. This is the single best Interrail value in Greece for most travellers.

When does the Interrail pass pay off in Greece?

A Global Flexi 4-day pass costs around €212 (adult). At €40–60 per long-distance journey in Greece, you need 4+ covered train journeys to break even on the Greek portion alone. The pass becomes worthwhile if you combine Greece with other Interrail countries — Balkans, Italy, or Austria — on the same trip.
