Keukenhof: How to Get There, When to Go, and Why You Should Arrive at 8am

7 million bulbs, 8 weeks a year, and a 90-minute window before the tour coaches arrive.

Netherlands — Lisse Day trip €20–37 entry Late March – mid-May only

I'll start with the thing I didn't expect: Keukenhof is much larger than it looks in photos. Every image you've seen was taken in the same 200-metre stretch near the main entrance. The actual park covers 32 hectares with 15 kilometres of walking paths, 4 indoor pavilions, a working windmill, a hedge maze, and 80 separate garden sections planted with 7 million bulbs. I went on a Tuesday in late April and arrived at 8:05, five minutes after opening. For the next 90 minutes I had the main beds almost entirely to myself. By 10:00, the first wave of tour coaches had arrived and the narrow paths near the entrance were shoulder-to-shoulder. The timing difference is that significant. Here's everything you need to know to make the most of it.

When Keukenhof is open — and the best weeks to visit

The opening window: late March to mid-May

Keukenhof opens for approximately 8 weeks per year, typically from the last week of March to the second or third week of May. Outside this window, the park is closed. The exact dates shift slightly year to year depending on the flowering calendar — check keukenhof.nl for the current season.

Peak bloom: which weeks are best

The tulip peak is usually mid-April to early May. Early season (late March–early April) has hyacinths, daffodils, and early tulip varieties — fewer crowds and cooler weather. Late season (first two weeks of May) has late tulips and less colour but the park is less packed. Mid-April is the peak for photos and the peak for visitors — if you have flexibility, the first week of May offers a good balance of colour and manageable crowds.

Timing

Weekday visits are noticeably quieter than weekends. If you're Interrailing through the Netherlands and have flexibility, a Tuesday or Wednesday visit in the first week of May is as close to ideal as you'll get.

Getting from Amsterdam to Keukenhof: two real options

Option 1: Keukenhof Express combo bus — €37, easiest

The Keukenhof Express is a direct bus service from Amsterdam Centraal and Amsterdam Leidseplein to the park gates. A combo ticket includes the return bus journey and garden entry: €37 total. No connections, no transfers. Journey time is approximately 40–50 minutes depending on traffic. This is the most convenient option and the one most visitors use. The bus starts early enough to arrive at 8:00 opening time if you take the first departure.

Option 2: Train to Leiden + Bus 854 — €20–25, cheaper

Take the intercity train from Amsterdam Centraal to Leiden Centraal (25 minutes, €7–9 each way depending on time of day and whether you have an NS Dagkaart). From Leiden Centraal, Bus 854 runs directly to Keukenhof during the Keukenhof season (April–May). Bus journey: approximately 30 minutes. Bus ticket: around €5 each way. Entry to the park is separate: €20.

Total cost return: approximately €20–25 excluding park entry, so around €40–45 all in. Slightly cheaper than the direct combo if you're buying NS train tickets separately. If you have an Interrail pass, the train leg is covered — meaning the total cost is Bus 854 both ways (€10) + park entry (€20) = €30.

Why arriving at 8:00 is the single most important thing you can do

Keukenhof opens at 8:00. Tour coaches — the large group tours from Amsterdam that carry 30–50 people each — typically arrive from 9:30 onwards. The window between 8:00 and 9:30 is transformative.

In that 90-minute window, you can walk the main tulip beds at the centre of the park in near-silence. The light is better (lower sun angle, softer shadows). The paths are empty enough to stop, frame a shot, and move on without someone walking through your frame. By 10:00, those same paths are narrow and crowded. By 11:00, the queues at the café inside are 20 minutes long.

Practical note

The Keukenhof Express first buses from Amsterdam leave early enough to arrive at or just after 8:00. Check the current timetable when booking — the first departure times shift slightly by year and month. The 8:00 opening time is consistent across the season.

What Keukenhof actually contains

Beyond the central flower beds that appear in every photo:

4 indoor pavilions

The Willem-Alexander Pavilion, Beatrix Pavilion, Oranje Nassau Pavilion, and the newly renovated Juliana Pavilion each have themed displays — competitive flower arrangements, historical tulip breeding displays, contemporary flower sculpture. These are worth at least 30–40 minutes combined and are a good rain escape if the weather turns.

Meermolen windmill

A working windmill at the edge of the park with a view over the tulip fields beyond Keukenhof's boundaries. The fields outside the park (bollenstreek) are visible from here — the commercial flower-growing fields that the park sits within. Worth the 10-minute walk to the far end of the park.

Hedge maze and children's area

A traditional hedge maze near the east section. Relatively quick to solve but a pleasant change of pace. The petting zoo (goats, rabbits) is nearby — relevant if you're travelling with children.

The 80 garden sections

Each garden section is planted by a different bulb grower — 80 in total — each with a different variety focus. The variety is genuine: some beds are monochrome blocks, some are mixed-colour arrangements, some focus on unusual varieties you won't see outside specialist gardens. Walk the full circuit rather than just the central beds.

Budget note

Food inside Keukenhof is expensive — €8–12 for a basic lunch, €4–5 for a coffee. Bring food if you're on a budget. There are picnic areas near the water. The on-site restaurant and café have the quality you'd expect from a monopoly operation.

Why most Interrail itineraries skip Keukenhof — and why that's a mistake

Generic Netherlands Interrail itineraries focus on Amsterdam, Rotterdam, and Utrecht. Keukenhof appears as a footnote or is omitted entirely. This makes sense year-round — for 44 weeks of the year it's closed and irrelevant. But if your travel dates fall in the late March–mid-May window, skipping Keukenhof in favour of another afternoon in Amsterdam is a genuine missed opportunity.

The tulip fields around the bollenstreek are a specific landscape that exists for 6–8 weeks a year. You're not going to encounter it anywhere else. The park is well-run, large enough to spend half a day, and — if you go early — not the crowd experience people fear. Factor it into your Netherlands timing if you can.

Frequently asked questions

When is Keukenhof open?

Keukenhof is open for approximately 8 weeks each year, typically from late March to mid-May. The exact dates vary slightly by year. Outside this window, the park is closed to the public. Check keukenhof.nl for the current season's exact open dates.

What is the cheapest way to get from Amsterdam to Keukenhof?

The cheapest route is train from Amsterdam Centraal to Leiden Centraal (€7–9 each way), then Bus 854 directly to Keukenhof (around €5 each way). Total return cost: approximately €20–25 plus €20 park entry. The direct Keukenhof Express bus combo from Amsterdam (€37 including entry) is more convenient but costs slightly more unless you have an Interrail pass covering the train leg.

What time does Keukenhof open?

Keukenhof opens at 8:00 during the main season. Tour coaches typically start arriving around 9:30. The window between 8:00 and 9:30 is the best time to walk the main flower beds without crowds. By 10:30 the most photographed areas are packed. Arriving at opening time makes a significant difference to the experience.

How long does Keukenhof take?

Plan for a minimum of 4 hours, ideally 5. Keukenhof covers 32 hectares with 15 kilometres of walking paths. There are 4 large indoor pavilions, a windmill, a hedge maze, and a petting zoo alongside the main flower beds. Rushing through in 2 hours misses most of it.
