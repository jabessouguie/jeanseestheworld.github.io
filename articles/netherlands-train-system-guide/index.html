The Dutch Train System for Foreign Visitors: NS, OVpay, and Getting Around

No reservations. Trains every 15 minutes. Tap your bank card and go. Here's how it all works.

Netherlands Year-round €8.50–€25 typical fares

The Dutch rail network is one of the most straightforward in Europe to use as a foreign visitor, and it's almost never explained properly in Interrail content. NS (Nederlandse Spoorwegen) runs trains between every major city every 15–30 minutes. There are no seat reservations to buy, no complicated booking windows, and since 2022, no need to buy a separate OV-chipkaart — you tap your contactless bank card directly at the entry gates and the fare is calculated automatically. I spent a week moving between Amsterdam, Utrecht, Leiden, Rotterdam, and Groningen entirely on NS trains and the GVB tram in Amsterdam. This is everything I learned about how the system actually works.

NS: how the national network works

NS (Nederlandse Spoorwegen) operates almost all intercity and regional train services in the Netherlands. The network connects every city in the country, with frequent services that run from early morning until after midnight on most routes.

No reservations required

This is the key difference from most of Europe. NS trains do not have seat reservations. You buy a ticket for the route and board any departing train in the appropriate class. If you miss a train, the next one leaves in 15–30 minutes. There is no such thing as "your specific train" on NS domestic services — the ticket is valid for any train on that route on that day.

Train frequency

On the main corridors, trains run every 15 minutes during the day:

  • Amsterdam Centraal – Utrecht Centraal: every 15 minutes, 26-minute journey
  • Amsterdam Centraal – Rotterdam Centraal: every 15 minutes, 40-minute journey
  • Amsterdam Centraal – The Hague (Den Haag): every 15 minutes, 50-minute journey
  • Amsterdam Centraal – Leiden Centraal: every 15 minutes, 35-minute journey

Groningen and Maastricht (longer distances) run every 30 minutes. Rural lines may run hourly.

OVpay: tap your bank card, forget about it

Since 2022, OVpay allows passengers to use a contactless bank card, phone, or smartwatch directly at NS station gates and GVB/RET tram/metro/bus validators. This replaced the old OV-chipkaart system for most tourists.

How it works

  1. Tap your contactless card at the NS gate when entering the station (or at the tram/bus reader when boarding)
  2. Travel to your destination
  3. Tap out when you exit the station (or when leaving the tram/bus)
  4. The system calculates the exact fare and charges your card within 24–48 hours

If you forget to tap out, the system charges the maximum fare for that region. Always tap out.

Which cards work

Visa and Mastercard contactless cards (including debit and credit) issued in Europe, the UK, and most other countries work reliably. Apple Pay and Google Pay are supported. American Express contactless has mixed results — bring a Visa or Mastercard backup. Maestro cards do not work with OVpay.

Tip

Use one consistent card throughout your Netherlands visit. OVpay links journeys to the specific card used for entry — mixing cards can occasionally cause issues with fare calculation on connected journeys.

What journeys actually cost

NS fares are distance-based and genuinely affordable by European standards. These are approximate second-class single fares:

Route Journey time Approx. fare (single)
Amsterdam – Utrecht 26 min €8.50
Amsterdam – Leiden 35 min €10
Amsterdam – Rotterdam 40 min €17.50
Amsterdam – The Hague 50 min €13
Amsterdam – Groningen 2h05 min €25
Amsterdam – Maastricht 2h30 min €28

A/R (return) fares on many routes offer modest discounts. The Dagretour (day return) is particularly useful: travel out and back on the same day for roughly 1.5× the single price, cheaper than two singles.

Two apps you actually need

NS app — for intercity trains

The official NS app (available in English) covers everything for national train travel: route planning, ticket purchase, live departure boards with platform numbers, and real-time delay notifications. It works offline for timetables. Buy tickets in the app and show your QR code at the gate — no need for a separate travel card for rail journeys if you prefer this to OVpay.

9292 app — for local transport

9292 is the authoritative door-to-door journey planner for all Dutch public transport: trains, trams, buses, metros, and ferries from a single interface. Use it when Google Maps gives you an uncertain result for a local GVB, RET, or HTM bus connection. 9292 shows exact vehicle numbers, platform assignments, and interchange times that Google Maps frequently gets wrong for the Netherlands.

Practical tip

Download both apps before arriving in the Netherlands. The NS app works offline for timetables, which is useful if your roaming data is unreliable on arrival. 9292 needs a connection for live routing but caches recent searches.

NS Dagkaart and group tickets: when they make sense

NS Dagkaart (Day Ticket) — ~€70

Unlimited second-class travel on all NS trains for one day. Worth buying if you're making 4 or more separate train journeys in a single day — for example, Amsterdam → Haarlem → Leiden → Rotterdam → Utrecht → Amsterdam. For 2–3 journeys, individual tickets cost less.

NS Weekend Free (Group ticket)

Available from Friday 19:00 to Monday 06:00. Minimum 3 people travelling together. Offers a significant group discount (around 40%) on a fixed day ticket. If you're travelling with 2–4 friends over a weekend, this ticket can be considerably cheaper than individual fares. Available through the NS app or station ticket machines.

Weekend tip

The NS Weekend Free group ticket can reduce per-person costs to below €15 for a full day of unlimited train travel across the Netherlands. Worth checking if your trip falls on a weekend with 3+ people.

Is Interrail worth using in the Netherlands?

This is the honest section. The Netherlands is one of the least economical countries in Europe for using an Interrail pass as a solo traveller, for three reasons:

  1. Fares are already low. Amsterdam–Rotterdam is €17.50. Amsterdam–Utrecht is €8.50. You're not saving much compared to other European countries where a single journey might be €40–60.
  2. No reservations required. One of the main practical conveniences of having the pass — not worrying about reservation fees — doesn't apply here because there are no reservations to buy.
  3. Short distances. Dutch cities are close together. A 7-day Netherlands itinerary might involve 8–10 train journeys, all short. The total cost without a pass might be €60–80 in train fares — less than the cost of adding one pass travel day.

If you already have a Global Pass for a broader European trip, using it on Dutch legs is fine — just note it doesn't save much. If you're considering buying a pass specifically for the Netherlands, the numbers don't work for solo travel.

The pass makes more sense if you're combining Dutch legs with adjacent countries (Belgium, Germany, France) on the same trip.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to reserve seats on Dutch trains?

No. Seat reservations are not available or required on NS domestic trains in the Netherlands. You buy a ticket and board any train in the right class on that route. Trains run every 15–30 minutes on all major routes. If one is full (rare), the next one is 15–30 minutes away.

What is OVpay and can I use my foreign bank card?

OVpay is the contactless payment system for Dutch public transport. You tap your contactless bank card or phone at the gate when entering and again when exiting. The system calculates the fare and charges your card automatically. Most major European and international Visa and Mastercard cards work. You do not need to buy a separate OV-chipkaart as a tourist.

What is the best app for travelling by train in the Netherlands?

The NS app is the primary app for intercity trains — buy tickets, check departure platforms, and see live delays. It works offline for timetables. For local journeys mixing tram, bus, metro, and train, use the 9292 app instead — it gives door-to-door routing across all transport providers and is more reliable than Google Maps for local Dutch transit.

Is an Interrail pass worth using in the Netherlands?

For most solo travellers: no. Dutch intercity fares are low — Amsterdam to Utrecht is €8.50, Amsterdam to Rotterdam is €17.50. Trains need no reservation. The Interrail pass only makes economic sense if you're making 4+ long trips within the Netherlands in a short period, or if you're combining Dutch legs with other European countries on the same pass. For a Netherlands-only trip, buying individual tickets or using OVpay will almost always be cheaper than spending a pass day.

What is the NS Dagkaart and when is it worth buying?

The NS Dagkaart (Day Ticket) costs approximately €70 and gives unlimited second-class travel on all NS trains for one day. It's worth buying if you're making 4+ separate train journeys in a single day. For 2 journeys, individual tickets are cheaper. For groups of 3+ travelling together on a weekend, the NS Weekend Free group ticket is more economical.
