Greek Cooking Classes: What to Book, Where & What They Actually Teach You

€60–100, 4–5 hours, moussaka to baklava — how to find a class worth your time

Athens & Crete 4–5 hours €60–100 Year-round

I have done two cooking classes in Greece — one in Athens that started at the Varvakeios central market, and one in a private home outside Chania where the host's grandmother supervised from a chair in the corner and occasionally took the spoon to correct us. The difference between them was the difference between a good experience and an excellent one. Both cost roughly €75. The Athens class taught me the fundamentals of moussaka. The Cretan class taught me that dakos is a construction problem and that Cretan olive oil has a peppery finish that makes the same recipe taste completely different. This guide is about how to find the second type of class, not the first, and why that distinction is worth about 5 minutes of research before you book.

Athens: market + kitchen format

The Varvakeios and Monastiraki market classes

The standard Athens cooking class format runs 4–5 hours and starts with a guided visit to a central market — either Varvakeios (the main meat and fish market near Omonia) or the smaller produce market at Monastiraki. You spend 45–60 minutes at the market with the chef, who explains how to choose ingredients: which olives, which feta (aged vs. fresh), what to look for in fish, how to pick herbs. Then you move to a kitchen and cook 3–4 dishes.

What you actually cook in Athens

  • Mezes: tzatziki, taramosalata, skordalia, or dolmades (varies by class)
  • One main dish: moussaka or pastitsio (layers of pasta, meat, béchamel)
  • A pastry: spanakopita (spinach and feta in filo) or tiropita (cheese pie)
  • Dessert: baklava (nut and honey pastry) or galaktoboureko (semolina custard)

Pricing

Expect €65–85 for a quality class. This includes the market visit, all ingredients, the full meal, and usually wine. Classes under €50 almost always mean the market portion is cut short or the group is too large.

Athens class tip

Book a morning class (9:00–10:00 start). The market is most active early, and you eat lunch after cooking — which is how the experience is supposed to feel. Evening classes do exist but the market component becomes a market walk rather than a shopping exercise.

Cretan cooking: more specific, more interesting

What makes Cretan cuisine distinct

Cretan cooking is not just Greek cooking with local ingredients — it has its own logic. The island uses more olive oil than anywhere else in Greece (Cretans consume an average of 20 litres per person per year), more legumes, more wild greens (horta), and fewer tomato-heavy sauces. Regional ingredients you will not find in the same form elsewhere: anthotiro (a fresh sheep cheese), Cretan honey (thyme-dominant), and aged graviera, which is used like parmesan.

Cretan dishes worth learning

  • Dakos: a barley rusk topped with crushed tomatoes, mizithra or anthotiro cheese, olives, and olive oil. Looks simple; the construction ratio matters more than you expect.
  • Boureki: a courgette, potato, and cheese gratin layered in a baking dish. Regional to western Crete (Chania area). Rarely found in Athens.
  • Staka: a rich butter made from sheep milk cream. Used in scrambled eggs (staka me avga) — one of those dishes that does not look like much and is completely addictive.
  • Kalitsounia: small fried or baked pies filled with sweet mizithra and honey, or with wild greens and herbs.

Where to find Cretan classes

Look for classes based in Chania, Rethymno, or the rural villages outside both towns. The best ones are run by local cooks (often women, often with family recipes going back generations) rather than by gastronomy schools or hotel programmes. GetYourGuide lists several; read the reviews with attention to group size and whether the host did the cooking or mostly supervised.

How to pick a good class and avoid a bad one

Group size is the key variable

In a group of 8–10 people, everyone has a task — chopping, stirring, rolling filo, plating — and you are actively cooking for most of the time. In a group of 15–20, you are watching someone else cook about 40% of the time. The €75 experience in the large group is worse than the €85 experience with 8 people.

Check whether the chef is the teacher

Some classes are operated by activity agencies who hire a generic chef on a contract basis. The better classes are taught by the person who designed the menu — a local cook, a restaurateur, or someone who learned from family. It shows in the explanations: a contract chef explains technique. A local cook explains why their grandmother added cinnamon to the meat sauce and why it is correct.

Ingredients should come from that morning

If you visit the market as part of the class, you should be buying the ingredients you will cook. If the ingredients are already prepped and on the counter when you arrive, the market visit was decorative. This is not automatically a dealbreaker, but it tells you something about the format.

Red flags summary

Groups over 12. Ingredients pre-portioned before you arrive. Chef listed as "professional guide" rather than a named local cook. Location in a hotel event room. Reviews mentioning "more of a demo than a class."

Frequently asked questions

How much does a cooking class in Greece cost?

Athens classes typically run €65–85 for 4–5 hours including a market visit and a full meal. Cretan classes cost €60–100 depending on group size and whether olive oil tasting is included. Budget classes under €50 usually mean large groups and shortcuts on ingredients.

What do you cook in a typical Greek cooking class?

Standard curriculum covers mezes (tzatziki, taramosalata, dolmades), a main dish like moussaka or pastitsio, a pastry like spanakopita or tiropita, and often a dessert like baklava or galaktoboureko. Cretan-specific classes add dakos, boureki, and dishes made with local olive oil and aged cheeses.

What is the difference between an Athens and a Cretan cooking class?

Athens classes are more general — they cover the Greek canon and often start with a tour of a central market. Cretan classes are more geographically specific, using regional ingredients like anthotiro cheese, Cretan thyme honey, and traditional dishes like dakos and boureki that are rarely found outside the island.

How do I spot a bad tourist cooking class?

Red flags: groups of 15+ people (you end up watching rather than cooking), ingredients prepped by staff before you arrive, chef who is an employee of a general activity agency rather than a local cook, and a location that looks more like a demonstration kitchen than a working home or restaurant kitchen.
