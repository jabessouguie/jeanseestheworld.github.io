Italy, by the forkful — Seven cities, fourteen meals

A 14-day north-south rail loop through Naples, Rome, Florence, Genoa and Milan, built around 15 specific restaurants and validated by an Italian whose palate I trust completely.

Italy / Southern Europe Easy 14 days ~€80/day Best: Apr–Jun, Sep–Oct Italy One Country Pass Gastronomy Solo Architecture Urban

What this is — and what it isn't

This isn't a list of the best restaurants in Italy. I'm not a food critic and I don't pretend to have eaten everywhere. What I have is a friend — Italian, grew up between Naples and Milan — who has never once steered me wrong on food. Every address in this guide passed through him first.

The route runs north-south by train: Naples (3 nights), Rome (3 nights), Florence (2 nights), Genoa (2 nights), Milan (2 nights), plus a day trip to Lake Como. That's 12 nights of sleep and a full two days of train travel, giving you 14 days total.

The focus is eating: what each city actually specialises in, where to eat it, how much it costs, and a few logistical notes that most food-travel guides skip. Prices are from my last trip and have been roughly verified — expect 10–15% variance.

Who this isn't for: if you want to tick off the Uffizi, the Vatican, and Pompeii in a single sprint, this route will feel too slow. I spend half my days wandering markets and eating a second lunch I didn't plan. If that sounds wrong to you, this guide isn't the right fit.

Trip overview

The route runs south-to-north — I prefer arriving in Naples first while energy is high, because Naples demands it. You can flip it if you're flying into Milan.

The right Interrail pass

For this specific route — 5 major high-speed legs in 14 days — the Italy One Country Pass is the practical choice over the Global Pass. You're staying entirely within Italy, so a single-country pass is cheaper and covers the same trains. The pass makes economic sense once you hit 4+ Frecce journeys: buying Super Economy tickets 3–4 weeks ahead (€19–29 each) can sometimes beat it, but only if you're flexible with exact departure times.

With a pass, each Frecce reservation costs ~€10. Five legs × €10 = €50 in reservation fees on top of the pass price. Point-to-point Super Economy for all 5 legs booked 3 weeks out: roughly €100–130 total. At that point the pass wins mainly if you're also doing spontaneous regional trains — which on this route, you will.

Italy One Country Pass — 4 travel days / 1 month
~€150–190 (adult, 2nd class)
4 travel days — covers all 4–5 main intercity legs; regional trains don't consume a day

When point-to-point beats the pass: if you book Italo (the private competitor) 3–4 weeks ahead, you can find Naples–Rome for €19 and Rome–Florence for €22. Interrail doesn't work on Italo — so a pass locks you into Trenitalia Frecce pricing. Do the maths on your specific dates before committing.

City by city

Days 1–3 — Naples (Napoli) · Campania

3 nights

Naples is the most exhausting and the most rewarding city on this list, in that order. The centro storico is loud, chaotic, and genuinely beautiful — and if you arrive without a plan for what to eat, someone will sell you something mediocre outside a church. Come with a list.

Pizza

Neapolitan pizza has two main formats: the classic round (dough fermented 24–48 hours, centre deliberately soft and floppy — this is not a defect), and pizza fritta, which is deep-fried rather than baked. The portafoglio ("wallet pizza") is a classic round folded in quarters — street food, €2–3, eaten standing. Do not sit at a table and order it.

  • Sorbillo — Antica Pizza Fritta da Zia Esterina, Via dei Tribunali 35: this is the pizza fritta branch, not the main sit-down Sorbillo. Crispy on the outside, ricotta and provola inside, ~€5. Queue moves fast. This is where you start Day 1.
  • Impasto 55 (@impasto_55 on Instagram for current address): modern Neapolitan pizza, quieter room, slightly more technique-forward. For when you want to sit and think about what you're eating.
  • La Masardona, Via Capitelli 27: the classic institution for pizza fritta. More no-frills than Sorbillo, same quality argument. Worth doing both and forming your own opinion.

Pastry and coffee

  • Poppella, Via Arena della Sanità 24: in the Rione Sanità neighbourhood, the fiocco di neve — a small brioche filled with chantilly cream and ricotta, dusted with powdered sugar — costs about €2. Eat two.
  • Neapolitan caffè con cremina is espresso with a cold foam made from the first drops of espresso and sugar, whipped by hand. It's not a cappuccino. Order it at any bar worth its equipment — just ask for "caffè con cremina".
  • Sfogliatella: the riccia (flaky, pastry-shell layers) is better than the frolla (shortcrust). Not all bars do both. The riccia should shatter when you bite it.

Dinner

  • Tandem Ragù, Via Giovanni Paladino 51: one dish. Neapolitan ragù — pork ribs, beef, sausage, slow-cooked for 8+ hours in tomato. The menu is essentially "how much pasta do you want with this sauce". Tiny room, impossible to walk in. Book via Instagram DM at least 3–4 days ahead.
Day trip: Pompeii (40 min by Circumvesuviana) Day trip: Sorrento (1h by Circumvesuviana) Day trip: Capri (ferry from Molo Beverello)
Tip

The Circumvesuviana to Pompeii is a separate regional network — your Trenitalia ticket or Interrail pass doesn't cover it. Buy the €3.60 ticket at the station. Keep an eye on your bag on this line.

Days 4–6 — Rome (Roma) · Lazio

3 nights

Rome's pasta canon is four dishes. Any Roman will tell you this. The argument is not about which is best — it's about whether you can eat all four in three days without being stupid about portion sizes. The answer is yes, but lunch has to count.

The four pasta classics

  • Carbonara: guanciale (cured pork cheek), egg yolks, Pecorino Romano, black pepper. No cream. No pancetta. No parmesan. The heat of the pasta cooks the egg — the pan goes off the heat before you add it. If it looks like scrambled eggs, it's wrong. Osteria da Fortunata, Via del Pellegrino 11: you can watch the pasta being rolled in the window. Solid, consistent carbonara.
  • Cacio e pepe: three ingredients — Pecorino Romano, black pepper, pasta water. No fat beyond what's in the cheese. The starch emulsifies the sauce. It's technically the hardest of the four to get right. Ai Spaghettari, Piazza San Cosimato 57, Trastevere: textbook execution, outdoor tables in the piazza when weather allows.
  • Amatriciana: tomato, guanciale, sometimes a pinch of chilli, bucatini or rigatoni. Lo Scopettaro, Lungotevere Testaccio 7: family-run, on the Tiber, no English menu, which usually means the right crowd is there.
  • Gricia: amatriciana without the tomato — just guanciale, Pecorino, black pepper, pasta water. The oldest of the four. Almost nobody outside Italy knows it. Order it whenever you see it.

Everything else

  • Pompi, Via della Croce 82: tiramisu in a plastic tray, classic or pistacchio or noisette or fragola. €5–7. The pistacchio one is why you're here.
  • Gelateria La Romana, Via Venti Settembre 60: a chain, but a serious one. The crema dal 1947 with fresh chantilly is the order. Don't overthink it.
  • Maritozzo: Roman breakfast brioche split open and filled with a mountain of fresh cream. Order it at any decent bar before 10:00.
Tip

Lo Scopettaro has no website and takes phone reservations only. Walk in early (12:30–13:00) or be prepared to wait. In three visits I've always gotten a table within 20 minutes of arriving — but not immediately.

Days 7–8 — Florence (Firenze) · Tuscany

2 nights

Two nights is tight in Florence if you care about art and food simultaneously. I made a decision early on: I go to the Accademia for the David (one morning, pre-booked slot), and I spend everything else eating, walking, and drinking Chianti at €2 standing in an alley. Museum marathons are for a different trip.

Schiacciata and sandwiches

  • I Fratellini, Via dei Cimatori 38: a hole in the wall, literally — the counter is a hatch in the wall. In business since 1875. About 20 types of schiacciata filled with different things (lardo, finocchiona, pecorino). Glass of Chianti for €2. You eat standing on the cobblestones outside. Queue in the morning, shorter at 14:30.
  • All'Antico Vinaio, Via dei Neri 65: the Instagram queue, the enormous sandwich. Is it worth it? Yes. Is the queue annoying? Also yes. Go at 11:30 before the main rush. The schiacciata bread is properly good and the portions are absurd.
  • La Prosciutteria, Via de' Neri 54R: a few doors from All'Antico Vinaio. Sit-down tagliere (charcuterie board) with Tuscan salumi and a carafe of Chianti. For when you want to eat slowly.

Bistecca alla Fiorentina

T-bone from Chianina cattle, cooked over wood, served very rare (al sangue — the inside should be deep pink to red). Minimum 1 kg, typically €60–90 for two people. You do not ask for it well-done. This is not negotiable.

  • Trattoria Mario, Via Rosina 2: lunch only, no reservations, communal tables, arrive at 12:00 (they open at noon, the queue forms at 11:45). Cash only. The bistecca here is the right price and the right atmosphere — chaotic, loud, exactly as it should be.
  • Trattoria dall'Oste / Il Latini, Via dei Palchetti 6R: prosciutto legs hanging from the ceiling, long communal tables, consistent fiorentina. More tourist-aware than Mario but still legitimate.

Evening at Piazzale Michelangelo

20-minute walk from Ponte Vecchio, uphill. The view over the city from the terrace is the best you'll get without paying for it. Bring a bottle of something from a nearby enoteca (€8–12), sit on the steps, and watch the light change. Go at 19:30 for the summer light, or 18:00 in spring.

Accademia (David)

Book online: €16 at the door, €20 + €4 booking fee online. The last slot is 18:20 — it's the quietest of the day. Since March 2026 there's a combo ticket with the Bargello available — worth it if you have a morning free.

Tip

Trattoria Mario accepts cash only and has no reservation system. If you arrive at 12:00 on the dot, you'll almost always get seated in the first wave. Arrive at 12:45 and you may wait 40 minutes. The bistecca takes 20+ minutes to cook — factor this in.

Days 9–10 — Genoa (Genova) · Liguria

2 nights

Genoa is not on most people's Italian itinerary, which is why I like it. The old port (porto antico) has been renovated into something actually pleasant, the caruggi (medieval alleyways) are genuinely atmospheric rather than performed, and the food is as regional and specific as anywhere in Italy. Nobody is making pesto alla genovese like this outside Liguria.

The three things to eat

Pesto alla genovese: fresh basil, pine nuts, garlic, Parmigiano Reggiano, Pecorino Sardo, extra-virgin olive oil, made in a marble mortar. Served traditionally on trofie (twisted short pasta) or trenette, with boiled potatoes and green beans in the same pot — this is not a mistake, it's the recipe. The version in Ligurian restaurants tastes nothing like the jarred stuff.

Focaccia: Ligurian focaccia is thin, very salty, soaked in olive oil, and eaten at breakfast — including, in Genoa, dipped in a cappuccino. This is real and correct. Don't overthink it.

Farinata: a flat pancake made from chickpea flour and olive oil, baked in a wood-fired oven. €2–3 per slice. Eaten with your hands. Goes cold fast — eat it immediately.

Addresses

  • Trattoria Sa Pesta, Via dei Giustiniani 16R: family-run since 1880. Trofie al pesto and farinata. Lunch only on weekdays, reservation required. This is the address my Italian friend gave me first and most emphatically.
  • Antica Sciamadda, Via San Giorgio 14R: wood-fired oven, farinata to take away. The queue is part of the experience and moves quickly.
  • Panificio Mario, Via San Vincenzo 61R: focaccia to go. Open from early morning. Buy a slab and walk to the port.
  • Cremeria Buonafede, Via Bartolomeo Bosco 31R: gelato near the Boccadasse neighbourhood (the small fishing village within the city). Pistacchio and fior di latte are the orders.
Day trip: Camogli (30 min by train, quieter than Cinque Terre) Day trip: Cinque Terre (90 min — avoid 11:00–16:00 in high season)
Tip

Cinque Terre from Genoa is doable but the trains between the five villages get very crowded from late morning. If you're going in May–September, take the first train and be in Riomaggiore by 9:00. Camogli is a better shout if you just want a quiet coastal town for a day — fewer people, same kind of food, easier logistics.

Days 11–12 — Milan (Milano) · Lombardy

2 nights

Milan is the easiest city on this list to eat badly in — there are more tourist restaurants per square metre around the Duomo than anywhere else I've been in Italy. The trick is going slightly off-centre and knowing what the city actually makes.

What Milan specialises in

  • Risotto alla milanese: saffron-yellow risotto, traditionally served alongside osso buco (braised veal shank). The combination is canonical; ordering risotto as a standalone primo is fine.
  • Panzerotti: a fried half-moon pastry filled with tomato and mozzarella, ~€3–5. Luini has been making them since 1949.
  • Aperitivo milanais: from 18:00 onwards, order a Negroni or Campari Spritz (Campari was invented here, in 1860, by Gaspare Campari at what is now Camparino in Galleria). Many bars include a spread of small snacks with the drink at no extra charge — this is the aperitivo ritual.

Addresses

  • Panzerotti Luini, Via Santa Radegonda 16: open Monday–Saturday, 10:00–20:00. The queue looks intimidating and moves in 10 minutes. Classic (tomato and mozzarella) is the reference; spinach and ricotta is, in my opinion, better. Get both.
  • Trattoria Masuelli San Marco, Viale Umbria 80: risotto alla milanese and osso buco as a duo, serious wine list with good Lombardy producers. Slightly formal, worth booking ahead for dinner.
  • Camparino in Galleria, Piazza del Duomo 21: yes, it's in the most touristy spot in Milan. Yes, it's genuinely the original Campari bar, with the original 1915 décor. One Campari Spritz or Negroni Sbagliato here is not a compromise — it's historical.

Aperitivo neighbourhoods

Brera (quieter, design crowd, good enoteca options) and Navigli (canal neighbourhood, livelier, more mixed). I prefer Brera for a drink before dinner; Navigli for an evening that might turn into a late one.

Tip

Luini is cash and card. The queue outside moves fast — don't be put off by the length. The panzerotti come out of the fryer in waves; if you arrive just as a batch has sold out, you'll wait 5 minutes for the next one. Worth it.

Day 13 — Lake Como (day trip from Milan)

Day trip

I was sceptical about Lake Como before I went. It felt like the kind of place that exists mainly for Instagram. It's more than that, but you have to go to Varenna instead of Bellagio to feel it.

The logistics

  • Train: Trenord from Milano Centrale to Varenna-Esino: 64 minutes, €7.40 each way, no reservation, trains every hour from 06:20 to 21:20. Sit on the left side after Lecco for the lake views. Do not use your Interrail pass for this — it counts as a travel day and costs more than the €7.40 ticket.
  • Varenna: smaller, quieter, less photographed than Bellagio. Villa Monastero has good gardens for €10. Lunch at Il Cavatappi or Quattro Pass al Lago — both have terrace tables over the water.
  • Ferry Varenna → Bellagio: ~15 minutes, €5 for a foot passenger. Bellagio has the cobblestone lanes, the gelato, and the villa Clooney. An hour is enough; two is comfortable.
  • Menaggio (same ferry network, other shore): quieter than Bellagio, less visited. An option if you prefer calm over postcard.
  • Last train from Varenna: 21:35. Budget under €60 for the whole day including lunch.
Tip

Guided tours of Lake Como from Milan cost €120–180 per person. The train and ferry DIY version costs €25–35 all-in and is more flexible. The only thing you lose is someone narrating the ferry crossing for you.

Getting around

Italy has two high-speed operators: Trenitalia (public, Frecce trains) and Italo (private). Interrail only works on Trenitalia. Regional trains (Trenord in Lombardy, Circumvesuviana in Campania) are separate networks with flat fares — no reservation needed, validate your paper ticket before boarding.

Where to stay

I stayed in mid-range hotels on this trip — nothing fancy, but private rooms with a bathroom. Hostels in Italian cities are genuinely good value and many have decent common areas. The neighbourhoods below are where I'd stay again.

Naples

Hostel: Centro storico or Spaccanapoli area — €25–40/night for a dorm, €65–90 for a private room. Stay inside the historic centre; commuting from outside adds friction. Mid-range: €80–120/night. Avoid anything near the train station (Piazza Garibaldi) unless you want to walk 20 minutes to reach the interesting parts.

Rome

Hostel: Trastevere or Testaccio neighbourhoods — €30–50/night dorm, €80–110 private. Both are well-connected and have good food options within walking distance. Mid-range: €100–160/night. Near the Vatican is convenient for that specific morning but otherwise a poor base.

Florence

Hostel: Oltrarno (south of the Arno) — €25–40 dorm, €70–95 private. More local feel than north of the river. Mid-range: €90–140/night. Anywhere within 15 minutes' walk of Ponte Vecchio works for this itinerary.

Genoa

Less developed hostel scene. Budget hotel: €55–80/night near the porto antico. Mid-range: €80–120/night. The city is compact; staying in the old centre means everything is walkable.

Milan

Hostel: €30–50 dorm, €90–120 private — Milan is expensive. Mid-range: €110–170/night. Navigli or Brera neighbourhoods give you good access to the aperitivo scene without being in tourist-hotel territory.

What to pack

Specific to this route. Italy in spring means variable weather: 15°C and rainy in Genoa, 25°C in Naples the same week.

  • A light layer for trains and churches: Italian churches enforce a shoulders/knees covered rule, enforced more strictly than you'd expect. Keep something in your bag, not your luggage.
  • Walking shoes that can handle cobblestones: Naples, Rome, and Florence all have serious cobblestone old towns. Running shoes work; sandals with thin soles don't, especially in rain.
  • A small tote bag: useful for carrying food from markets, wine for Piazzale Michelangelo, panzerotti from Luini. Every Italian seems to have one.
  • Cash (€50–100): Trattoria Mario is cash only. Several street food places are cash preferred. ATMs work everywhere but carry some buffer.
  • Screenshot the Instagram pages of Tandem Ragù and Impasto 55 — neither has a stable address on Google Maps, and you need them offline in the historic centre where signal can be patchy.
  • Sunscreen for the Lake Como day: the ferry and Varenna waterfront have zero shade. Easy to underestimate a lake day in summer.
  • Rail Planner app (offline): download the Italy timetable before you leave. Wifi in Italian train stations exists but is unreliable.

Honest take

My take

Italy is the country I've returned to most on Interrail trips, and it's also the easiest to do badly — the gap between a mediocre tourist restaurant and a genuinely good one is narrow in price and enormous in experience. Having a local friend as an advisor changed how I eat here completely.

What surprised me: Genoa. I went because it was logistically convenient between Florence and Milan, and I ended up staying an extra half day. The farinata alone would justify the detour.

What could be better: I don't love the Frecce reservation system. You're paying €10 per leg on top of a pass that already cost €170, and the seats aren't always meaningfully better than what you'd get on an Intercity for €3. The pass math works out, but it's not elegant.

Would I repeat it? Already planning the southern extension — Sicily for the arancini and the granita, and Emilia-Romagna for the mortadella and the proper ragù. That's a different trip. This one stands on its own.

Frequently asked questions

Is the Interrail Italy Pass worth it for this itinerary?

Yes, if you do 4+ long-distance Frecce or Italo journeys in 2 weeks. The Italy One Country Pass covers Naples–Rome, Rome–Florence, Florence–Genoa, Genoa–Milan — that's 4 high-speed legs where point-to-point could easily run €120+ total versus ~€40 in Frecce reservation fees on the pass. Regional trains (Trenord to Lake Como, trains around each city) don't require pass activation, so you preserve travel days.

When should I avoid travelling in Italy?

Avoid August. Restaurants close for chiuso per ferie (summer holiday), particularly in smaller cities. The heat in Naples and Rome regularly reaches 36–40°C. The best windows are April to early June, or mid-September through October. I did this trip in May and had no issues with closures.

Do I need to book restaurants in advance in Italy?

It depends on the place. Tandem Ragù in Naples requires booking via Instagram — without it you won't get in. Trattoria Mario in Florence has no reservations at all (arrive by noon). Trattoria Sa Pesta in Genoa takes reservations for lunch on weekdays. I'd say 3 out of the 15 addresses on this list genuinely require planning ahead; the rest you can walk in.

How do I validate train tickets in Italy?

Regional trains on paper tickets must be validated (obliterato) in the yellow machines on the platform before boarding — not doing so is a fine, even if the ticket is valid. High-speed Frecce and Italo tickets are nominative and tied to a seat, so no validation needed. Interrail pass: activate the travel day in the Rail Planner app before the journey and show it on your phone.

What's the difference between Trenitalia and Italo?

Trenitalia is the public operator (Frecce high-speed, Intercity, and regional). Italo is a private competitor running high-speed trains on major corridors like Naples–Rome–Florence–Milan. Italo is often €15–30 cheaper on the same route, especially if you book 3–4 weeks out. The catch: Interrail passes are not valid on Italo. If you have a pass, book Trenitalia Frecce. If you're going point-to-point, always check Italo first.

Can I do the Lake Como day trip on an Interrail pass?

The Trenord train from Milan Centrale to Varenna-Esino (€7.40 each way, 64 minutes) is a regional train. It counts as a travel day on the pass, which makes no sense economically — just buy the point-to-point ticket at the machine. Total out-of-pocket for the day including Varenna ferry is under €25.

What if my Trenitalia train is more than 30 minutes late?

You're entitled to 25% of the ticket price back as a Trenitalia voucher (bon). Submit the claim directly in the Trenitalia app within 12 months. It's not cash, but it stacks up if you're doing multiple legs. Italo has a similar policy.
