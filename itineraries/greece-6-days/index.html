6 Days in Greece by Interrail — Athens, Olympus, Meteora, Crete

4 regions, 2 cooking classes, 1 canyon walk, 0 rental cars. Total cost: ~€1,230 flights excluded.

Greece Moderate 6 days ~€205/day Best: May–June, Sept Global Flexi 4-day Food Hiking Culture Solo

Why this route exists

Every Greece guide on the internet is built around the same five blue-domed photos and a ranked list of rooftop bars. This one isn't.

I spent six days in Greece without renting a car, without visiting Santorini, and without once setting foot in Mykonos. Instead: a food crawl through Monastiraki, a cooking class in Kerameikos, a car-free hike to Zeus's Bath on Mount Olympus, a guided day trip to Meteora that was objectively the best thing I did all week, an overnight ferry to Crete, a second cooking class with a family in the hills above Chania, and a 16km canyon walk to the Libyan Sea. The total was €1,230 flights excluded. That's honest mid-budget, not "I survived on bread" optimised.

What makes this route different from standard Greece content is the combination of mainland and Crete with no car, sequenced in a way that makes logistical sense. The Interrail pass covers the two key mainland legs (Athens to Katerini for Olympus, Athens to Kalambaka for Meteora). The ferry to Crete is separate. KTEL buses cover everything on the island. No one in this itinerary needed to rent a Fiat Panda.

Who this is not for: people who want to party in the Aegean, people who need a beach day every 48 hours, anyone who'd rather drink cocktails at sunset than eat dakos in an alley. No judgment — but this is a different trip.

Trip overview

Athens on days 1 and 4 (arrival and departure), with Mount Olympus and Meteora as excursions in between, then Crete for the final two days. The overnight ferry does double duty as transport and accommodation.

The right Interrail pass

This itinerary uses 3 rail travel days: Athens to Katerini (for Olympus), Katerini back to Athens, and Athens to Kalambaka (for Meteora — return is handled by the day trip). A Global Flexi 4-day pass gives you one extra day of buffer, which is useful if you add Thessaloniki or take a different return leg.

Global Flexi — 4 travel days / 1 month
€212
4 travel days / 30 days validity

When point-to-point beats the pass: if you're only doing Athens + Meteora as a day trip (no Olympus overnight), 2 train days may not justify €212. In that case, a Greece One Country Pass (up to 8 travel days, ~€124) is the smarter buy. The pass does not cover the Piraeus–Crete ferry — that's €70–130 extra depending on whether you want a cabin.

Reservations: Greek trains require a free seat reservation made at the station counter at least one day before. The conductor will ask for the slip. No reservation, no seat guarantee even with a valid pass.

Day by day

Day 1 — Athens

1 night

I came to Athens for the food first and the ruins second. The Acropolis isn't going anywhere.

Morning: food tour through the markets (~9am–12pm)

I booked the Athens Greek Foodie small-group tour through GetYourGuide. Three hours, seven or eight stops, ending at the central market. Our guide was a 60-something Athenian woman who openly judged the tourist trap pita places we walked past. Felt like being shown around by a strict-but-loving aunt. The loukoumades at the final stop were the best I've had in my life, and I grew up eating them.

Do this on day one, not day three. You'll learn which neighbourhoods to come back to.

Afternoon: cooking class in Kerameikos (~2pm–7pm)

The class was in a restored stone house in Kerameikos. Eight people, five solo travellers, three couples. The teacher, Dimitra, opened with: "if you finish this class without staining your shirt, you weren't paying attention." We made spanakopita from scratch, tzatziki, slow-roasted lamb, chocolate-and-olive-oil mousse. Wine flowed. I left with a stained shirt as instructed. It felt more like a cozy dinner party than a class. Best decision of my Athens stay.

Evening: dinner in Koukaki or Pangrati

Avoid Plaka after 7pm unless you enjoy menus translated into eight languages. Walk 15 minutes to Koukaki or Pangrati — both residential neighbourhoods with proper local tavernas. No reservation needed before 9pm. Dinner for one with wine: €20–30.

Heads up — Acropolis tickets

From April to October, the Acropolis uses time-slot tickets. Book online the day before, choose 8am, arrive at 7:55. The site at 8am is empty. The site at 11am is a Disneyland queue with no shade. Entry is €20. This is not optional advice.

Tip — free views

Half the rooftop bars in Plaka charge €15 for a Mythos. The Areopagus rock at sunset is free. Lycabettus Hill costs one cable car ticket. Don't pay for the view, pay for what's in the glass.

Day 2 — Mount Olympus via Litochoro

1 night

Mount Olympus, mythological home of the Greek gods, is the highest mountain range in Greece. The peak (Mytikas, 2,917m) is climbable from mid-June to October with crampons. The lower trails are accessible year-round and that's where I went, because I'm not Hercules. Most travel content tells you to rent a car. I refused.

Step 1: Train Athens (Larissa station) → Katerini (~4 hours)

Three departures daily: roughly 8am, 12pm, and 6pm. ~€15–25 one way. With an Interrail pass the train is included — reserve a seat at the station counter the day before (free, mandatory). Take the 8am to maximise hiking time.

Step 2: Bus Katerini → Litochoro (~30 min, €2)

15-minute walk from Katerini train station to the KTEL bus stop. Bus runs roughly every hour during the day. The return bus is where things get unreliable — see the warning below.

Step 3: Trail to Zeus's Bath (Loutra Dios)

From Litochoro main square, the marked trailhead is 5 minutes' walk. Zeus's Bath is a 1-hour easy walk in shaded forest along a fresh mountain stream. The full Enipeas gorge trail (out and back, ~4 hours) is the better option if you slept in Litochoro. Switchback paths through pine forest, exposed limestone cliffs, the river always somewhere below you. Easy gradient, properly marked. In May I saw maybe twelve other hikers in 4 hours. 22°C and breezy at the trailhead. There are deeper swimming holes if you brought a quick-dry towel. I did not. Regret.

Warning — the bus might not come back

My return bus from Litochoro to Katerini did not show up. I waited 35 minutes, called a taxi (€15, 15 minutes). Don't bank on the schedule. Either build in 90 minutes of buffer or pre-arrange a taxi from your guesthouse before leaving. Local Bolt coverage is very spotty.

Tip — sleep here, don't day-trip

The last train back to Athens leaves around 5pm. Doing this as a day trip from Athens means a brutal 4pm Litochoro turnaround. Sleep one night in Litochoro (guesthouses €50–70, get anything with a mountain-facing balcony), do the longer E4 trail in the morning, take the late-morning train back. The lamb chops at any wood-fired taverna in Litochoro are some of the best in Greece for the price. This alone justifies the overnight.

Day 3 — Meteora (day trip from Athens)

Day trip

Meteora ("suspended in the air" in Greek) is a complex of six functioning Orthodox monasteries built between the 14th and 16th centuries on top of vertical rock pillars. Monks used rope ladders and pulleys to get up there for hundreds of years. They still live there. They refused to let Game of Thrones and a James Bond crew film on site. I respect them deeply.

Train vs. day trip

Athens to Kalambaka is 4h30–5h by train, €15–25 one way. As a day trip from Athens, that's 9–10 hours of train for 4–5 hours on site. Mathematically dumb. I booked a guided Meteora day trip through GetYourGuide that handled the round-trip from Athens, included a Greek lunch in a village taverna (the moussaka was life-changing — the French bistro version should be illegal), and stopped at four of the six monasteries. The guide knew which monastery had the best frescoes versus which had the best view, and which one had a 90-year-old nun who would judge your shoulders.

Which monasteries to visit

  • Great Meteoron: the biggest, most touristy, has the museum and old monastery kitchen. Worth it for the scale.
  • Varlaam: best frescoes. Smaller, calmer — you can have the chapel to yourself for 5 minutes if you arrive at opening.
  • Roussanou: the most photogenic from the outside. Run by nuns, smaller inside.
  • Holy Trinity: the famous one on the thinnest pillar. Long staircase. The James Bond one. Worth the climb.

All six rotate closed days through the week. Check the day before or you'll arrive at a locked gate.

Dress code — enforced, no exceptions

Long skirts for women (wraps provided at most entrances, free). Long trousers for men. Shoulders covered for everyone. They will not let you in. They've heard all the arguments. Pack one pair of light long pants that doubles as hiking and monastery-appropriate.

If you have time — sleep in Kastraki

If you add a night, wake up at 5:30am, walk to the panoramic viewpoint above Kastraki, and watch the sun rise behind the rock pillars. Zero crowds. The Instagram day-trip people miss this entirely. Their loss.

Day 4 — Athens to Crete (overnight ferry)

Ferry night

Travel days have a bad reputation. I disagree. Morning photoshoot, afternoon nap, sunset ferry departure from Piraeus. One of my favourite days of the trip.

Morning: professional photoshoot (~9am–10:30am)

I booked a one-hour pro photoshoot through GetYourGuide. The photographer met me at the Lycabettus viewpoint, then walked me through Plaka and Monastiraki. ~€100, 60 photos delivered three days later. As a solo traveller, your phone selfies will not capture this trip the way you want them to. I felt slightly silly clicking "book" and very glad when I got the gallery back. Consider it.

Book on GetYourGuide

Athens: Professional Photoshoot (1 hour, multiple spots) — search "Athens Professional Photoshoot" on GetYourGuide.

Afternoon: nothing after 4pm

The ferry leaves Piraeus around 9pm. Be at the terminal by 7:30pm. Athens metro Line 1 to Piraeus is €1.20 and drops you 5 minutes from the terminals. Use the afternoon to pack, eat a real dinner, and nap.

The ferry itself

ANEK Lines and Minoan Lines both run Piraeus–Souda (Chania). Round-trip: €70–90 for a deck seat, €130–180 for a 2-bunk inside cabin. Get the cabin if you have any intention of sleeping. The deck bar serves Mythos until midnight. The ferry arrives Souda around 6am.

Tip — arrival in Chania

Souda is the port; Chania old town is a 15-minute taxi ride away (€15) or public bus (€2, runs from 6:30am). Guesthouses won't check you in until 11am — leave your bag at reception and walk straight to the Venetian harbour for breakfast. The morning light on the 16th-century lighthouse at 6:30am is one of the better views in Greece.

Day 5 — Chania, Crete

1 night

My cooking teacher in Athens told me Cretan food is the best in Greece. I thought she was exaggerating. She was not. Crete has its own personality — the dialect is different, the food is heavier on lamb and wild greens, and they drink raki the way the French drink water.

Morning: walk the old town (before 11am)

Chania's old town is one of the most photogenic places in Greece, and almost no tourist content covers it properly. The Venetian harbour has a 16th-century lighthouse at the end of a long stone breakwater. The alleys behind the harbour are crumbling Ottoman houses with yellow walls and bougainvillea. Walk it slowly, ideally before 11am or after 6pm to dodge the heat. The Splantzia neighbourhood is the best part — green-shuttered townhouses, locals actually living there.

Lunch: To Steki

Tucked in an alley behind the harbour. Yellow walls, painted bougainvillea, ten tables. Order: the dakos (rusk with tomato and mizithra cheese), the lamb staka (slow-cooked in cream and butter — terrifying and perfect), and a half-litre of house white. Lunch for one with wine: around €25.

Afternoon: Cretan cooking class (~2pm–8pm)

The class I took was at a family-run kitchen in the hills, 20 minutes' drive from Chania (transport included). Six people. The teacher's grandmother — the actual Cretan yiayia — sat in the corner, occasionally yelling cooking corrections in Greek that the daughter translated as "she says you're chopping too slowly." We made dakos, stuffed grape leaves, a slow-cooked lamb in egg-and-lemon sauce I now think about weekly, and bougatsa for dessert. The wine was from the family's vineyard. Three hours of cooking, two hours of eating, ninety minutes of dancing in the kitchen. I went in solo, left full, tipsy, and with five new friends, plus a homemade olive oil stain on my shirt. Worth every euro.

On raki

Cretan raki is not Turkish raki and not Greek ouzo. It is a clear grape-pomace spirit, 40–45%, served in tiny glasses, ice optional, on the house at the end of every meal in Crete. It is considered rude to refuse the first one. By the third, you stop pretending you have plans.

Day 6 — Samaria Gorge

Full day

Samaria Gorge is the longest gorge in Europe: 16km, almost entirely downhill, 1,200m of descent, ending in a coastal village called Agia Roumeli that you can only leave by boat. UNESCO biosphere reserve. Open early May to mid-October. This is the best final day Greece can offer.

Why I booked a guided excursion

You can do Samaria independently, but the logistics are genuinely painful. You need a 5am bus from Chania to Omalos (the trailhead), a pre-booked return ferry from Agia Roumeli to Sougia or Hora Sfakion, and a return bus from there to Chania — all coordinated. Miss one connection and you're sleeping in a coastal village with no plan B. I booked the GetYourGuide full-day Samaria trek: pickup at 5:45am from central Chania, bus to Omalos, guide for the first kilometre to set the pace, then you walk at your own speed, ferry pre-booked at the bottom, return bus to Chania. Got back to the guesthouse around 7:30pm, exhausted, satisfied, full.

What the hike actually feels like

First 2km: steep switchback descent into the canyon. Wooden stairs, loose gravel. Your knees will know about it tomorrow. Take your time. The view at the second viewpoint, looking down into the canyon mouth, is the best of the entire hike.

Middle 10km: flat-ish riverbed walking. You cross the river roughly 15 times via stepping stones. The canyon walls close to 4 metres apart at the Iron Gates (Sideroportes), with 300m-high walls on either side. Ranger huts with water roughly every 4km. Refill liberally — it gets hot by 11am.

Final 4km: through the abandoned village of Old Samaria (basic taverna, cold drinks), then 2km more to Agia Roumeli on the Libyan Sea. Most people swim before the ferry.

Warning — it is harder than advertised

"All downhill" is technically true and practically a lie. 16km of uneven rocky riverbed is brutal on quads and knees. Trail runners or proper trail shoes only — not Vans, not Converse, not flip-flops. I saw someone in flip-flops at km 5. She was crying. Don't be her. Bring 2 litres of water minimum at the start, refill at ranger huts.

Where to stay

Mid-range hostels in Athens and Chania, one guesthouse night in Litochoro, and the overnight ferry as a moving bedroom. No budget for a private villa, but no need for one either.

Athens — Athens Hawks Hostel

25 Menandrou St, 5 minutes from Omonia metro. The block is gritty in an "actual working-class Athens" way that makes some people nervous and others happy. The hostel is the opposite: clean, well-staffed, real lockers with USB charging inside the dorm, 24/7 reception (I rolled in at 6am after the Crete ferry return and got coffee while waiting). Rooftop bar is a real one. Solo-friendly without being a frat house. Pub crawl exists if you want it.

  • Dorms: €25–35/night
  • Private doubles: €60–80/night
  • Book direct at athenshawks.com for cheapest rate.

Litochoro — any mountain guesthouse with a balcony

Small mountain village, 15 minutes from the trailhead. Plenty of options in the €50–70 range. Pick anything with a balcony facing Mount Olympus — the morning view is worth the extra €10. Eat at any taverna with a wood-fired oven; the lamb chops here are some of the best in Greece for the price.

Chania — Boho City Hostel

22 Theotokopoulou St, a narrow Venetian alley 2 minutes from the harbour and 4 minutes from To Steki. Family-run since 2008 by Angelika, who runs it the way a Cretan grandmother runs a kitchen: warmly and with strong opinions. Wood-panelled bunks with privacy curtains, reading light per bed, communal kitchen, small outdoor sitting area. Unbeatable location for the price. Owners run side trips (boat excursions, lesser-known gorges) that are cheaper than anything on aggregators.

  • Dorms: €25–40/night
  • WhatsApp Angelika at +30 6938 767 438 — faster than Hostelworld and sometimes cheaper.

What to pack

One carry-on backpack covers this entire trip. You are hitting Athens (city), Mount Olympus (forest hike), Meteora (stairs + monastery dress code), and Crete (16km canyon + beach + tavernas). The specific things people get wrong for this route:

  • Trail runners, not hiking boots: You need shoes that work on the Olympus trail, the Meteora paths, and Samaria Gorge. Trail runners beat boots here — lighter, they dry faster, and you don't need a separate "city" pair. This matters a lot on the Samaria Gorge. I cannot stress enough: do not attempt Samaria in Vans, Converse, or flip-flops.
  • A long skirt or light long pants: Mandatory for Meteora monastery entry. Women need knees covered. Men need long trousers. Wraps are provided at most entrances but they're ugly and shared. One pair of light convertible hiking pants solves the monastery, the trails, and warm evenings in Chania.
  • €100 in small bills before leaving Athens: Rural tavernas around Litochoro, the bus ticket in Katerini, and one specific monastery (I won't name it) still operate cash-only. ATMs exist in Litochoro but not at the trailhead.
  • A 20L daypack: Your main bag stays at the hostel. Samaria Gorge and the Olympus trail both need a daypack. 20L fits 2 litres of water, sunscreen, a snack, and a rain layer.
  • Refillable 1L water bottle: Tap water in Greece is drinkable in cities. Ranger huts on Samaria have water every 4km. A refillable bottle saves €15 over six days and 12 plastic bottles.
  • Sunscreen SPF 50 and a hat that doesn't fly off: The Enipeas gorge trail and Samaria both have long exposed sections. Mid-May sun in Greece is not French April sun. Ask me how I know.
  • EU power adapter (Type C/F): One adapter is enough. Leave the surge-protected travel hub at home — you don't need it.
  • A quick-dry towel: The swimming holes on the Olympus trail and the beach at Agia Roumeli (end of Samaria) both reward this. I forgot mine both times. Learn from my regret twice.

Honest take

My take

I'd do this trip again, almost identically, with two changes: I'd sleep two nights in Litochoro instead of one (the morning on the E4 trail is something the day-tripper version completely misses), and I'd add one more day in Chania before the Samaria walk — my legs were not fully recovered from Olympus when I did the gorge on day 6. The Meteora day trip was the single best decision of the week. The cooking class in Crete was the best meal.

What sucked: the Litochoro bus not showing up (real, annoying), Athens summer crowds at the Acropolis even at 8am in May (do the 8am slot anyway — it's much better than 11am, just not empty), and the fact that the Samaria Gorge ferry connection at the bottom is non-negotiable — if you're tired and slow, you're still making that boat.

What surprised me: how little Meteora appears in serious travel content relative to Santorini, given that it's objectively more extraordinary. Greece is bigger and weirder than the Instagram version suggests. Use the pass, take the slow ferry, eat at the place with no English menu.

Frequently asked questions

Is an Interrail pass worth it for 6 days in Greece?

Yes, if you do the mainland legs. Athens to Katerini (for Olympus, ~€20) and Athens to Kalambaka (for Meteora, ~€22) already come close to the cost of a Global Flexi 4-day pass (€212). The ferry to Crete is not covered by the continental pass — you pay that separately (€70–130 depending on cabin vs. deck). The Interrail Greece One Country Pass is a cheaper alternative (~€124) if you're only staying in Greece and doing more train days.

Can I do this Greece itinerary without a rental car?

I did it without a car and I'd do it again. Athens is metro and Bolt. Mount Olympus requires train to Katerini then bus (or taxi, €15) to Litochoro. Meteora is easiest via guided day trip from Athens. Crete works on KTEL buses around Chania. The one place the car-free approach requires patience is Litochoro — the return bus is unreliable. Build in 90 minutes of buffer or pre-arrange a taxi (get a number from your guesthouse before leaving).

When should I go to Greece to do this itinerary?

May to early June or late September. Samaria Gorge opens early May and closes mid-October. Mount Olympus lower trails are accessible year-round but the peak (Mytikas, 2,917m) needs crampons and is only viable mid-June to October. July and August are oven-mode — Samaria in 38°C is a different experience from the same hike in 24°C. Prices also peak in August.

How much does 6 days in Greece actually cost?

My total was ~€1,230, flights excluded. That's: ~€450 accommodation (5 nights mid-range), ~€180 internal transport (trains, ferry, buses, taxis), ~€340 experiences (2 cooking classes, Meteora day trip, photoshoot, Samaria trek), ~€180 food and drink outside experiences, ~€80 miscellaneous. Budget ~€205/day mid-range. You can cut €150–200 by skipping the photoshoot and doing Meteora independently by train instead of guided day trip.

Is Samaria Gorge hard?

Harder than advertised. "All downhill" is technically accurate and practically misleading. 16km of uneven rocky riverbed with 1,200m of descent is brutal on quads and knees. Trail runners or proper trail shoes are non-negotiable. Do not attempt in Vans, Converse, or flip-flops — I saw someone try with flip-flops at km 5. She was crying. The logistics (5am bus, pre-booked ferry from the bottom) are the bigger headache, which is why a guided excursion from Chania makes more sense than going independently.

What is the dress code for Meteora monasteries?

Strict and enforced. Women: long skirt covering knees (wraps provided at most entrances at no charge if you forgot), shoulders covered. Men: long trousers, no tank tops, shoulders covered. They will not argue with you. They've heard all the arguments. Six monasteries rotate closed days through the week — check the day before or you'll arrive at a locked gate.

What is the overnight ferry from Athens to Crete like?

Ferry leaves Piraeus around 9pm, arrives Souda (Chania) around 6am. ANEK Lines and Minoan Lines both run the route. Deck seats cost €70–90 return; a 2-bunk inside cabin costs €130–180. Get the cabin if sleeping matters. Athens metro Line 1 to Piraeus is €1.20. Be at the terminal by 7:30pm. The ferry is cheaper than flying and you save a night of accommodation — one of the better value moves in Greek travel.
