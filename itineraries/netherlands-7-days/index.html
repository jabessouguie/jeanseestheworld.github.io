7 Days in the Netherlands by Interrail — The Honest One-Week Guide

Amsterdam as a base, 4 day trips, 8 cities covered — and the only thing I rented was a bike for €12.

Western Europe / Netherlands Easy 7 days ~€80/day Best: April–May Global Flexi 4-day Solo Architecture Nature Urban

Why this route

Most Netherlands guides tell you to see Amsterdam in 2 days and then move on to Belgium. That's fine if you want to tick a box. But the Netherlands is small enough that you can stay in one hostel, one locker, one neighbourhood — and use the country's excellent rail network to reach entirely different landscapes each morning. That's what this week is about.

Amsterdam rewards people who get up early. The canals at 7am, before the tour boats load up, before the cheese shop queues form, are genuinely calm and beautiful. The city is not calm at 2pm on a Saturday in July. That context matters for everything in this guide.

What makes this route different: you're not hauling your bag between 7 hostels. You're based in Amsterdam for days 1–3, doing real day trips from day 4 onward, and making one choice on day 7 based on your mood. NS trains leave every 15–30 minutes to anywhere you'll want to go. There are no reservations required on domestic Dutch trains. OVpay means you tap your bank card at the gate and go.

This itinerary is not for you if: you want to drive through tulip fields at your own pace, you're travelling with a large family with luggage, or you need to cover the whole country in one trip. This is a focused, train-based week for one or two people who are comfortable navigating independently.

The right Interrail pass

For 7 days based in the Netherlands, the Global Flexi 4-day / 1 month pass is the most practical option — but only if you're arriving from the UK or France by Eurostar. That international leg uses 1 Interrail travel day each way (2 days total for a return). The remaining 2 travel days can cover any two other international hops if you extend the trip into Belgium or Germany.

If you're only travelling within the Netherlands, 7 days of trains cost around €60–80 total in point-to-point tickets — well below the pass price. Save Interrail travel days for the international legs only. The Eurostar reservation fee is €30–40 on top of any pass.

Day by day

Day 1 — Amsterdam: arrival and orientation

Night 1 of 6

Arrive at Amsterdam Centraal — either direct from Schiphol (17 minutes by intercity, €5.70) or straight off the Eurostar platform which deposits you directly into the station. Check in to Hans Brinker on Kerkstraat. It's a 12-minute walk from Centraal through the Singel canal ring, which is a useful orientation loop.

Day 1 is for decompression and neighbourhood reconnaissance. Walk the Jordaan: the grid of streets west of the main canals between Prinsengracht and Westerpark. The Noordermarkt runs on Saturdays (8am–4pm, secondhand clothing and organic produce). The Lindengracht market runs Saturdays too. No entry fees, no queues, just streets.

For dinner: skip the Dutch food entirely on night one. The Surinamese and Indonesian options around Albert Cuypmarkt and in the Pijp neighbourhood are €7–12 for a full plate. Roti with vegetables at a Surinamese snackbar will cost less than any tourist restaurant near the Rijksmuseum. The Netherlands colonised a lot of the world and the food situation reflects that, in the best possible way.

Tip

Get a 24-hour GVB public transport ticket for €8.50 from the machines at Centraal. It covers trams, metro and buses. The OVpay contactless system works too, but the day ticket is better value if you're doing more than 3 tram rides.

Day 2 — Amsterdam: museums, early

Night 2 of 6

Set an alarm for 8am. The Rijksmuseum opens at 9am and the first hour — before tour groups arrive — is the only time you can stand in front of Rembrandt's The Night Watch without 40 people in your frame. Ticket: €22.50. The Rijksmuseum Library (free, separate entrance on the south side) is underrated — a working research library inside a stunning 19th-century reading room. You can sit in it for free.

After the Rijksmuseum, walk 8 minutes east to the Hortus Botanicus (€11.50). It's a botanical garden founded in 1638, one of the oldest in the world. The palm house is warm even in April. Give it 90 minutes.

Afternoon: the Moco Museum on Museumplein (€21). I'll be direct — it's an Instagram factory with genuine taste. The Banksy rooms are well-curated, and the building itself (Villa Alsberg, 1904) is better than most venues showing contemporary art in this city. If that combination sounds cynical to you, skip it. If you're curious about how street art translates to a white-cube setting, it's worth the 2 hours.

Tip

The I Amsterdam City Card (€65 for 24h, €85 for 48h, €105 for 72h) covers Rijksmuseum entry, Moco Museum, Stedelijk, the canal cruise and all public transport. If you're doing 3+ museums plus transport in a day, the 24h card breaks even. For days 2–3 combined, the 48h card saves around €25.

Day 3 — Amsterdam: canals and the Stedelijk

Night 3 of 6

By day 3 you've earned a slower morning. Walk along the Prinsengracht from the Anne Frank House (book online in advance if you want to go in — €14, timed entry, sells out weeks ahead) north to the Westerkerk tower (€9, 85m, worth it for the views over the canal rings). Or just walk. The canal views from the bridges on Reguliersgracht, where 7 bridges align in a row, are free.

Afternoon: the Stedelijk Museum of Modern Art (€20, included with City Card) has a permanent collection that covers De Stijl through Mondrian and includes a strong design section. It's less crowded than the Rijksmuseum and the building's 2012 extension — nicknamed "the bathtub" — is interesting from outside.

Evening: the Albert Cuyp Market (Mon–Sat, 9am–5pm) is done by now, but the Pijp neighbourhood has the densest concentration of good cheap restaurants in the city. Indonesian rice table (rijsttafel) for 2 at a sit-down place runs €30–45 total with drinks. Worth it once.

Tip

Amsterdam rewards people who get up early. Every museum, canal and market is better before 10am. The city's tourist infrastructure is genuinely strained in summer afternoons — plan accordingly rather than fighting it.

Day 4 — Keukenhof day trip

Night 4 in Amsterdam

Open only late March to mid-May. If you're not travelling in that window, substitute this day with Haarlem (20 minutes west, free to walk, excellent Frans Hals Museum at €17.50).

Keukenhof is 7 million bulbs planted across 32 hectares. The scale is real — photos do not convey it. The GetYourGuide combo (code JEANSEESTHEWORLD5, gyg.me/jeanseestheworld-app) includes the bus from Amsterdam and entry for around €27–30 total, versus €22.50 entry alone plus separately buying bus tickets.

Leave Amsterdam Centraal by 8:30am — there are direct Keukenhof Express buses from outside the station. Arrive at 9am when it opens. The pavilions fill up by 11am. I spent 4 hours there and had seen everything worth seeing by hour 3. The outdoor sections are better than the indoor flower shows unless you're specifically interested in competitive horticultural display.

Back in Amsterdam by 2pm, which leaves the afternoon free for the Albert Cuyp Market or a canal bike (€15/hr, locks near Leidseplein).

Tip

Peak bloom is the third week of April, when tulips, daffodils, hyacinths and narcissi overlap. If you're going in late March or early May, check the Keukenhof website's live bloom tracker (they post it weekly). A trip at 30% bloom is not worth €27.

Day 5 — Zaanse Schans day trip

Night 5 in Amsterdam

Zaanse Schans is 20 minutes from Amsterdam Centraal by Sprinter train to Koog-Zaandijk (€3.80 single, so under €8 return with OVpay). Walk 15 minutes from the station, or take the free ferry that runs seasonally.

It's a reconstructed 18th-century village with working windmills, a cheese farm, wooden shoe workshop and period houses. I'll be honest: it's a tourist attraction that does exactly what it says it will. The windmills are functional and genuinely impressive in scale — you can go inside one for €5. The cheese tasting is free with a light sales pitch at the end. The wooden shoe demo is 8 minutes long and free.

The Zaans Museum (€16) covers the history of industry along the Zaan river and has a Monet room — he painted the windmills here in 1871. Worth it if you're interested in the industrial history. Skippable if you're not.

Back in Amsterdam by 1pm. Spend the afternoon doing anything you missed from days 1–3, or sit in the Vondelpark with a baguette from Albert Heijn (€1.89).

Tip

Zaanse Schans is free to enter as an area. The paid attractions inside (windmill, museum) are optional. You can have a perfectly good 3-hour visit for the cost of the return train ticket. Arrive before 10am to see the site without crowds — tour buses arrive from 10:30am onward.

Day 6 — Utrecht

Night 6 in Amsterdam

Utrecht is 25 minutes direct from Amsterdam Centraal by intercity train (€8.50 single, every 15 minutes). It's the Netherlands' fourth-largest city and the one that feels most like a European city rather than a tourist product.

The Oudegracht (Old Canal) is unique: it runs through the city centre with a lower level of wharf cellars that have been converted into bars, restaurants and galleries. You're walking on street level looking down at people eating lunch in what used to be medieval storage vaults. It's one of the more genuinely distinctive urban spaces I've found in northern Europe.

The Dom Tower is the tallest church tower in the Netherlands at 112m. Guided tours only (€12, 45 minutes, about 465 steps). Book online — it sells out on weekends. The Dom Church itself (free) is connected to the tower by a covered passageway and the interior is spare, Protestant and interesting for its medieval architecture.

The Centraal Museum (€15) has the original De Stijl furniture by Gerrit Rietveld and a good collection of Dutch Golden Age work. Better in some ways than what's in Amsterdam for this period.

Eat in the canal cellar bars on Oudegracht for lunch — sandwiches and soup run €8–12. For dinner back in Amsterdam or in Utrecht before the last train (runs until midnight).

Tip

Utrecht has a strong student population and the bars and coffee shops are considerably less tourist-facing than Amsterdam. If you want a drink in a place where locals also go, this is easier to achieve in Utrecht than in most of Amsterdam's centre.

Day 7 — Choose your final destination

Final day

Three options for your last day, all reachable from Amsterdam by NS train without reservations:

Option A — Groningen (2 hours north, €22 single)

The Reitdiephaven harbour is photogenic in a way that doesn't require any effort to appreciate: coloured houseboats, old warehouses, a walking path. The Groninger Museum (€17) is a postmodern building designed by Alessandro Mendini that's as interesting outside as in. The city centre has the Vismarkt and Grote Markt squares and a low density of tourists. Rebel Rebel hostel (€32–45/night dorm) is boutique and calm during the week.

Option B — Rotterdam (1 hour south, €14 single)

Rotterdam was bombed flat in 1940 and rebuilt from scratch, so it looks like no other Dutch city. The Markthal (free to enter, market stalls inside a horseshoe arch of apartments with a painted ceiling) is one of the better recent buildings in the country. The Cube Houses (Kubuswoningen, €3 to enter one) are exactly what they look like: houses tilted at 45 degrees. The Erasmus Bridge over the Maas is best seen from the south bank at sunset. Hostel ROOM (€28–40/night) is functional and central. Return to Amsterdam in time for your departure.

Option C — Maastricht (2.5 hours south, €27 single)

The most different option. Maastricht is in a narrow strip of the Netherlands between Belgium and Germany and the culture reflects that — French is spoken, Belgian beers are sold, the food is heavier and better. The Vrijthof square is where most people end up. The Roman ruins underneath the Basilica of Saint Servatius (€5) are genuinely Roman, not reconstructed. The bookshop inside the Dominicanen church (free to browse) is the most visited attraction. If you're flying from Eindhoven or Liège the next day rather than Schiphol, Maastricht makes sense as a final night.

Tip

For day 7, check your departure logistics before choosing. If you're leaving from Schiphol, Rotterdam or Amsterdam are easier returns. If you're flexible, Groningen is the least touristy and the most authentic Dutch city experience of the three.

The Netherlands has the best domestic train network I've used in Europe for short-distance travel. No reservations required on any NS train. Trains run every 15–30 minutes on all major routes. Where to stay

The base strategy here is staying in Amsterdam for all 6 nights, which keeps costs down and eliminates the logistics of moving between cities. Amsterdam hostels are good value relative to hotels, and the location of your hostel matters less than you think — trams connect the whole city for €2 per ride.

Amsterdam — Hans Brinker Hostel

Type: Large social hostel  |  Price: €28–45/night in dorm, €85–120 private
Location: Kerkstraat, 15 minutes walk from Centraal, central to Rijksmuseum and Leidseplein
Vibe: Festive, very social, common areas are genuinely active. Not a hostel for light sleepers. The bar downstairs runs until late.

I stayed here for 6 nights and the lockers are secure (bring a padlock), the showers are clean and the breakfast (€6, buffet style, 7–10am) is worth paying for when you're heading to Keukenhof at 8:30am and don't want to think about food logistics.

What to pack

Specific to this route — the things people actually forget.

  • A padlock for hostel lockers — Hans Brinker and most Amsterdam hostels do not provide them. A TSA-approved combination lock costs €6 at any Action store in the Netherlands (there's one near Centraal).
  • A light waterproof layer — not a full rain jacket necessarily, but something with a hood. April in Amsterdam is 12–15°C with regular rain showers. A thin shell that packs small is more useful than an umbrella on a bike.
  • Good walking shoes — Amsterdam's cobblestones are uneven and slippery when wet. Trainers work. Flip-flops do not.
  • A reusable water bottle — tap water in the Netherlands is among the best in Europe. Every café will refill your bottle. Don't buy bottled water here.
  • A bike lock if you plan to rent frequently — some rental shops provide one, many don't. A small D-lock is worth having for the €12 daily rental bikes near Vondelpark.
  • Download NS and 9292 apps before you arrive — NS is the main train app (real-time departures, platform changes, digital tickets). 9292 is for multimodal routing including buses and trams. Both work offline for pre-downloaded journeys.
  • Sunscreen for Keukenhof — the tulip fields are open ground with no shade. An April day with sun reflects off the flowers in a way that will surprise you.
  • Your bank card that supports contactless OVpay — Visa and Mastercard work. Some older Maestro-only cards do not. Check before you go.

Honest take

My take

The Netherlands is the most logistically easy country I've done by train. Every connection works, the platforms are signed clearly, and OVpay means you never queue for a ticket. That convenience is also the downside: because it's easy, it's crowded with people who are also doing it easily. Amsterdam in July is too much. In April before 10am, it's genuinely worth being there.

What surprised me: the food. I expected bland northern European cooking and instead found the best Surinamese food outside of Suriname and an Indonesian rijsttafel tradition that's actually maintained with care. Skip anything marketed as "traditional Dutch" near tourist areas. Eat where the postal codes are high and the menus have Papiamento on them.

What I'd skip: the Red Light District as a walking tour destination — it's a crowded commercial space that functions fine but doesn't need tourist rubbernecking. Schiphol airport if you can use Rotterdam The Hague or Eindhoven instead — smaller, faster, less aggressively commercial. And any cheese shop within 200 metres of the Rijksmuseum.

Would I repeat this week? Yes, but in April. The tulip timing is real, the weather is manageable, and the city hasn't yet hit its summer capacity ceiling.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need an Interrail pass for the Netherlands?

For a 7-day trip based in Amsterdam, a Global Flexi 4-day pass (€212) makes sense if you're arriving by Eurostar — that leg alone uses 1 travel day each way. Within the Netherlands, NS trains run so frequently and cheaply (Amsterdam–Utrecht €8.50, Amsterdam–Zaanse Schans under €8 return) that paying point-to-point for domestic legs is often better value than activating a travel day.

When is the best time to visit the Netherlands?

April and May are the most practical months. Keukenhof is only open from late March to mid-May, tulip fields are visible along roadsides, and temperatures reach 15–18°C. July and August are more crowded and hotter, with Amsterdam streets packed to an uncomfortable degree. September is good for cycling and lower crowds.

Do I need an OV-chipkaart for Dutch trains?

No. Since 2023, NS trains accept contactless bank cards directly via OVpay. Tap in, tap out at the yellow readers with any Visa or Mastercard. No OV-chipkaart needed for visitors. The NS app also lets you buy single tickets in advance. For Amsterdam trams and metro, OVpay works at the gate readers.

Is Amsterdam safe for solo travellers?

Yes, in general. The Red Light District at 2am is its own environment — crowded, chaotic and relentlessly tourist-facing — but the rest of the city is calm. The main risks are bike theft and pickpockets around Centraal Station. Keep one bag in front of you in crowds, and lock a rented bike with two locks (the rental shop's lock plus your own).

How do I get from Paris or London to Amsterdam?

Eurostar from London St Pancras to Amsterdam Centraal is direct, around 4 hours, from £39 if booked 6 months ahead. From Paris Gare du Nord it's 3h20 via Thalys/Eurostar, from €29 booked early. Both trains require a mandatory reservation on top of any Interrail pass, costing €30–40. Book at eurostar.com directly — third-party sites often charge extra booking fees.

Is Keukenhof worth the price?

At €22.50 entry plus around €5 for the bus combo, it's not cheap. Whether it's worth it depends entirely on timing. Go in the third week of April when all varieties peak simultaneously. Arrive at 9am when it opens — by 11am the paths are congested. The GetYourGuide combo (code JEANSEESTHEWORLD5, gyg.me/jeanseestheworld-app) includes bus and entry and saves queuing at the ticket desk.

Should I visit Rotterdam or Groningen for my 7th day?

Rotterdam (1 hour south) is better if you're interested in architecture — the Markthal, Cube Houses and Erasmus Bridge are worth 5 hours. Groningen (2 hours north) is better if you want to experience a Dutch city that isn't built around tourism. Maastricht is the longest journey (2.5 hours) but feels almost Belgian and has the most distinctive food scene of the three.
