10 Days in Croatia by Interrail — The Honest Guide

3 bases, 1 pass, 5 stops: Zagreb to Split via Plitvice, with islands, a day in Dubrovnik, and prices that will surprise you — not always pleasantly.

Balkans / Croatia Moderate 10 days ~€90/day Best: mid-June or mid-Sept Global Flexi 7-days/1-month Solo Beaches Nature History Islands

Why this route

Croatia gets oversold as a cheap Balkan destination. It isn't, at least not on the coast in summer. What it actually is, is one of the most varied 10-day rail trips you can build on an Interrail pass: a lived-in capital with a functioning tram system and a genuinely odd museum, a UNESCO national park that justifies every cliché thrown at it, a Roman palace you sleep inside, and a string of islands that make the ferry money feel well spent.

This itinerary uses 3 bases — Zagreb, then Split — and treats Plitvice and Dubrovnik as day trips rather than overnight stops. That choice saves roughly €100 in accommodation alone. The route runs north to south, which means you arrive on the train network and leave from Split with easy onward connections to Bosnia, Montenegro, or back through Slovenia.

Who this isn't for: anyone wanting a beach holiday with minimal moving. If you're happy spending 5 days in one spot on the coast, a point-to-point ticket from Zagreb to Split and a rental apartment probably serves you better than this. This route suits solo travellers who want to cover ground, don't mind a 6-hour train, and are willing to accept that Dubrovnik in August is more of a sociological study than a holiday.

The right Interrail pass

On this 10-day route I use 4–5 actual rail travel days: Ljubljana → Zagreb (day 1), Zagreb → Split (day 4), Split → Zagreb (day 9), and optionally Zurich → Zagreb overnight (travel day straddles two calendar days, counts as 1 travel day). That puts the Global Flexi 7-days-in-1-month comfortably in range — it gives you 2–3 travel days in reserve for deviations.

The Split–Dubrovnik Flixbus is not covered by the Interrail pass and costs ~€30 each way. Budget that separately.

Day by day

Day 1 — Arrival in Zagreb

1 night

Most people arrive from Ljubljana (2.5h by train, Interrail valid, reservation recommended at ~€3) or overnight from Zurich (13h, couchette, ~€20–30 reservation on top of the pass). Both routes drop you at Zagreb Glavni Kolodvor, which is walkable to the upper town.

Day 1 is a soft landing. Drop your bag at Chill Out Hotel (central, rooftop bar, club downstairs if you want it), walk up Ilica Street to Gornji Grad, find Zrinjevac park for a coffee, and call it an early night. You have 9 more days.

Tip

On the overnight from Zurich, book the couchette in advance — they sell out in summer. A travel day starts at midnight, so the overnight train only uses 1 travel day on your pass if you board after midnight, or 2 if you board the previous evening (check the Rail Planner app to confirm which calendar day activates).

Day 2 — Plitvice Lakes + Rastoke (day trip from Zagreb)

1 night (Zagreb)

This is the best day of the trip, full stop. Plitvice Lakes National Park sits 2 hours south of Zagreb and there is no sensible way to reach it without a car or an organised tour. I booked through GetYourGuide (use code JEANSEESTHEWORLD5 for a discount) — the tour runs €60–80 and includes a stop at Rastoke village on the way down: a cluster of watermills built directly over cascading streams that most people skip entirely because it's not in the park itself.

At Plitvice, go against the crowd flow — start from Entrance 2 and walk the upper lakes first. The park recommends Route H (4h, 18 km) for a full day; you cross the largest lake by boat midway. The water is an unreal turquoise-green caused by dissolved minerals, not filters.

The park entry fee is included in most tours. If you go independently: €35–40 in summer. Wear shoes you don't mind getting wet — the boardwalks flood in rain.

Tip

Bring a packed lunch from Zagreb. Food inside the park is expensive and slow. Rastoke has a family-run restaurant by the mill wheels — worth stopping if the tour schedule allows it.

Day 3 — Zagreb

1 night

Zagreb deserves more than people give it. Most visitors spend half a day before rushing to the coast. I spent 3 nights using it as a base and left wishing I had more time.

Museum of Broken Relationships (Gornji Grad, ~1h, ~€6): donations of objects from ended relationships, each with a short text. It sounds gimmicky. It isn't. One of the more affecting museums I've been in on this whole trip.

Lotrščak Tower: the noon cannon fires every day at 12:00 from the tower above the funicular. It costs nothing to stand below it. The tower itself costs €2 and gives a good city view.

Funicular: 66 metres, 55 seconds, claimed to be the shortest public funicular in the world. Costs €0.53 one way. Worth it for the novelty even if you'd normally walk the 5-minute path alongside.

What to skip: Dolac market is fine but overrated for tourists — you'll pay tourist prices. Ban Jelačić Square is unavoidable as a transit hub but requires nothing from you. Spend the afternoon in Zrinjevac park instead and watch locals play chess.

Tip

The 24h Zagreb Card (€18) covers all tram rides and discounts on museums. Worth buying on Day 3 if you're doing the tower and the tram to the lower town repeatedly. Skip it if you're mostly walking — the city is compact.

Day 4 — Zagreb → Split (train)

Travel day + 1 night (Split)

The Zagreb–Split train takes 6 hours minimum, often longer. It runs once a day in the morning (check current timetable — schedules shift seasonally). Reservation is mandatory and non-negotiable — you cannot board without one. Book at the station counter or through Rail Planner; the fee runs €8–12.

Practical notes: no restaurant car, no power sockets. Pack a substantial meal, charge everything the night before, download something to watch. The interior views through the Lika region are actually very good — karst hills and river valleys — but that won't save you after hour 4.

Arrive in Split mid-afternoon. Walk straight from the station into Diocletian's Palace — the Roman emperor built his retirement palace here in the 4th century and the city grew up inside its walls. The streets inside are narrow, often unmarked, and genuinely confusing for the first 20 minutes. That's the point: just walk.

Evening on La Riva (the waterfront promenade) after dinner — avoid the tourist restaurants with laminated menus directly facing the water and walk 2 streets back for half the price.

Tip

There is a direct Flixbus Zagreb–Split that takes about the same time and can be cheaper (€15–25). It's not covered by the Interrail pass, but if you're short on travel days it's a reasonable alternative. Check both options before committing.

Days 5–7 — Split (base)

3 nights

Split rewards slower exploration. The palace district (Grad) rewards walking in the morning before the cruise ship groups arrive — by 11 am the Peristyle courtyard fills up. The Peristyle itself is the central open space of the palace, flanked by columns and featuring a small Egyptian sphinx (yes, a real one, brought over by Diocletian). You can stand there for free.

Marjan Hill: 20 minutes on foot from the palace. A forested peninsula above the city with views over the bay and the islands. Go at golden hour. Take water.

Beaches from Split on foot: mediocre. Bačvice is the most famous (a tiny arc of sand famous for a local game called picigin played in ankle-deep water) but it gets packed by 10 am in August. The good swimming is on the islands — which is what Day 6 is for.

Tip

Staying at Hostel Fiesta Siesta Social is a legitimate choice for solo travellers — the staff organise beach days and evening outings and the social dynamic is one of the best I've encountered in a Croatian hostel. If you want quiet, book elsewhere.

Day 6 — Island-hopping from Split (Blue Lagoon, Vis, Hvar)

Day trip from Split

Best day on the coast. The full-day boat tour "Blue Cave, Vis, Hvar & 5 Islands from Split" covers more ground in 10 hours than you could do independently in 3 days, and the snorkelling stops are at spots you simply wouldn't find on your own.

Itinerary (varies by operator): Blue Lagoon (translucent water, anchor and swim) → Vis / Komiža (a small fishing town, 1h free time, coffee and burek) → Hvar town (2h free time, climb up to the Španjola fortress in 20 minutes for a panoramic view — don't bother with the town's bars which charge Ibiza prices).

Cost: €100–130 per person via GetYourGuide (use code JEANSEESTHEWORLD5). Snorkelling gear included. Bring your own water — drinks onboard are expensive and a single water bottle in Hvar costs €5.

Important note on Hvar: the island is extremely expensive to overnight on in August (€60+ for a dorm bed, €30+ for a main course). Day-tripping from Split is the right call unless you have a specific reason to stay.

Tip

Book this tour 3–5 days in advance in July–August — it regularly sells out. Pay the extra €10–15 for a small-group option (max 12 people) over the large catamaran tours. The difference in experience is significant.

Day 8 — Dubrovnik day trip from Split

Day trip from Split

Flixbus from Split to Dubrovnik: 4–5 hours, ~€30 each way, departs from the main bus station (5 minutes from the ferry terminal). Not covered by Interrail. Book both legs before you leave Split — checking the return bus time before you depart sounds obvious, but I didn't, and almost missed the last one.

City walls (€35): do them at opening (8:00) or after 17:00. In August midday the walls hit 38°C and there's no shade. The walls are genuinely impressive — 2km loop, towers, sea views — but at €35 they're one of the more expensive things you'll do in Croatia. Worth it once; I wouldn't go back.

Lokrum Island (ferry from the old port, ~€15 return, every 30 min): pine forest, a monastery, swimming spots, and free-roaming peacocks. Much calmer than the old town. Go in the afternoon when the wall crowds are thickest.

Game of Thrones locations: the old town was used extensively for King's Landing. The walking maps are free and easy to follow. The Jesuit Staircase (Red Keep stairs), Fort Lovrijenac (Red Keep exterior), and the Rector's Palace are all within 10 minutes of each other.

Verdict: Dubrovnik in August is overrated as a base. As a day trip it earns its place in the itinerary. Go in June or September and the calculus changes entirely — the city is genuinely one of the most architecturally coherent in Europe.

Tip

Dubrovnik enforces a daily visitor cap on the old town and the walls. Buy the wall ticket online the day before — walk-up queues in August are 45–60 minutes. The cap doesn't affect walking in the old town itself, just the walls.

Days 9–10 — Split → Zagreb → departure

1 night (Zagreb)

Same train back north (6h, mandatory reservation, same rules as Day 4). Arrive Zagreb early evening — the city doesn't shut down at 9 pm, unlike most of the coastal stops. Last dinner in Gornji Grad.

Option for Day 10: rather than flying from Zagreb, add a Ljubljana stopover on the way home — the 2.5h train north reconnects you to the Slovenian rail network and gives you an evening in one of the most underrated small capitals in Europe. Works well if your onward flight is from Ljubljana airport.

Tip

Zagreb airport (Franjo Tuđman) is 30 minutes from the centre by bus (€5, departs from the main bus station every 30 min). Don't book a taxi from the arrivals hall — three times the price of the shuttle.

Where to stay

Two cities, two very different hostel scenes. I'll give one hostel and one mid-range option per city.

Zagreb

Chill Out Hotel (hostel) — €25–35/night (dorm): rooftop bar, basement club, central location in Donji Grad. Sociable but can be noisy late. Good for solo travellers who want to meet people.

Mid-range alternative — €70–100/night: a private room in the Gornji Grad area or near Ilica Street puts you central without the hostel noise. Booking.com gives good options in the €70–85 range outside high summer.

Split

Hostel Fiesta Siesta Social — €30–40/night (dorm): energy, beach outings, organised evenings, and genuinely sociable staff. Best option in Split for solo travellers who don't want to navigate the social scene alone.

Mid-range alternative — €80–120/night: a studio apartment inside or adjacent to the palace walls. Sleeping inside a Roman emperor's palace retirement home is worth the price premium once.

What to pack

Specific to this route — not a generic packing list.

  • Powerbank (20,000 mAh minimum): the Zagreb–Split train has no power sockets. 6+ hours without charging kills your phone navigation and your entertainment simultaneously.
  • Snorkelling mask: the island tour includes gear but the shared masks are often a bad fit. Bringing your own lightweight mask makes the Blue Lagoon stop noticeably better.
  • Water sandals / aqua shoes: the Plitvice boardwalks flood, several Split beaches are rocky, and the boat tour involves stepping off into shallow water. Crocs or equivalent.
  • Dry bag (10L): for the boat tour day. Your phone, wallet and passport should not be in a cotton tote on a moving boat.
  • Reusable water bottle: water on the Hvar waterfront costs €5. Tap water in Croatia is drinkable everywhere. Fill up before boarding any boat tour.
  • Light long-sleeved layer: the Zagreb–Split train is air-conditioned to meat-locker temperatures regardless of outside heat. Also useful for the Dubrovnik walls at 8 am when there's sea wind.
  • Burek for the train: buy a portion at a Zagreb bakery before you board. Genuinely one of the better ways to spend €3 in Central Europe, and you'll need the calories.
  • Rail Planner app (offline mode): download Croatia and Slovenia timetables before you leave home. Croatian signal on the Zagreb–Split route drops out for long stretches.

Honest take

My take

Croatia is better than the hype and more expensive than the hype, at the same time. Plitvice is genuinely worth the full-day detour from Zagreb — no asterisk, no qualification. The Split–islands day was the coastal highlight I'd do again without hesitation. Dubrovnik in August is a crowd-management exercise dressed up as a holiday, and I say that having enjoyed it: the walls are real, the old town is architecturally coherent, but at €35 entry and €5 water bottles you feel the machinery of mass tourism quite acutely.

The one concrete mistake I made: I didn't check the return Flixbus time from Dubrovnik before booking the outward ticket. Last bus back leaves earlier than you think. Fix that before you go. Would I repeat this trip? Yes, in mid-September, not August.

Frequently asked questions

Is Croatia expensive for a solo traveller on an Interrail pass?

More expensive than people expect. The coast (Split, Dubrovnik) runs at Italian prices in summer — €40+ for a sit-down meal, €5 for a bottle of water in Hvar. Budget €70–90/day on the coast realistically. Zagreb and the interior are much cheaper. August is the worst month for both price and crowds; mid-June or mid-September cuts costs by roughly 20–30%.

Does the Interrail pass cover the Zagreb–Split train?

Yes, and you must activate a travel day for it. You also need a mandatory seat reservation — book it through the Rail Planner app or at the station counter. The reservation fee is around €8–12. The journey takes 6 hours or more. Bring food and a powerbank: no restaurant car, no power sockets.

Can I visit Dubrovnik as a day trip from Split instead of sleeping there?

Yes, and in August I'd argue it's the smarter move. A Flixbus from Split takes 4–5 hours and costs around €30 each way — the Interrail pass does not cover this leg. Sleeping in Dubrovnik in August costs €60+ for a dorm bed and the city is packed. Do the walls at 8 am, cross to Lokrum island in the afternoon, and take the evening bus back.

Do I need a car for Plitvice Lakes from Zagreb?

Not if you book an organised tour from Zagreb. Most tours (€60–80 via GetYourGuide) include a stop at Rastoke village on the way, drop you at the entrance, and pick you up after 4 hours. Without a car or a tour, the bus from Zagreb takes about 2.5 hours and drops you at the park gates — doable but the tour adds real value because of the Rastoke stop.

Which Interrail pass is right for 10 days in Croatia?

The Global Flexi 7-days-in-1-month pass (€312 adult) is the right fit. You'll use 4–5 rail travel days on this route (Ljubljana–Zagreb, Zagreb–Split, and optionally Zurich–Zagreb overnight). The Split–Dubrovnik leg is Flixbus and doesn't consume a travel day but also isn't covered. Point-to-point tickets for just Zagreb–Split can run €40–60, so the pass pays for itself if you're arriving from further afield.

Is August a good time to visit Croatia?

Honestly, no — not on a budget. Accommodation prices spike, coastal restaurants add summer surcharges, and Dubrovnik hits 35°C with wall-to-wall crowds. I went in August and I'd go back in mid-September: the sea is still warm, prices drop noticeably, and you can actually walk around Hvar without queuing for a table.

Are the beaches in Split worth it?

The beaches within walking distance of Split centre (Bačvice, Kaštelet) are small and crowded. The good swimming is on the islands. Book the island-hopping boat tour from Split — it covers Blue Lagoon, Vis/Komiža and Hvar in one day for around €100–130 and includes snorkelling stops that are genuinely worth the money.
